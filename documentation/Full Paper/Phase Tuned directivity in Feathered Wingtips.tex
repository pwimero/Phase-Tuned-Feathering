\begin{abstract}
Feathered wingtips and other segmented lifting-surface terminations are often interpreted as acoustic phased arrays, but directivity changes can also arise from source-strength redistribution, local dipole rotation, decoherence, and radiation filtering. This paper develops a theory-only radiating-covariance framework for distinguishing these mechanisms. The far-field spectrum is written as a positive-semidefinite quadratic form, $S_{pp}=\vect{a}^{H}\mat{C}_q\vect{a}$, and then decomposed into independently radiating source-covariance eigenmodes, $S_{pp}=\sum_r \lambda_r|\vect{a}^{H}\vect{u}_r|^2/(4\pi R)^2$. This identity shows that geometry alone does not determine directivity control; control depends on how source-covariance modes project onto the acoustic radiation operator. Exact computational validation over 1000 randomized covariance cases gives no PSD, Hermitian, modal-identity, or off-diagonal-bound failures. Canonical benchmarks recover diagonal no-phase behavior to $5.8\times10^{-16}$ relative error and a rank-one coherent array factor to $5.6\times10^{-16}$ normalized error. A 625-case partial-coherence sweep produces a mechanism phase diagram in which phase-dominant behavior appears only when geometric phase separation and radiating off-diagonal covariance are both present. Reanalysis of the 50-target feathered-wingtip campaign shows that optimized full-model geometries retain substantial fixed-geometry phase sensitivity ($\overline{\mathcal P_R}=0.811$, phase-ablation RMSE $7.93\,\mathrm{dB}$), while zero-coherence reoptimization remains statistically equivalent to the full model. Thus phase interference is active but not uniquely controlling in the tested regime: the design space can compensate through alternative source-covariance and radiation modes. The released FWA-Bench-0 package provides exact, canonical, transition, and feathered-wingtip benchmark cases for reproducing the computational validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Structured lifting-surface terminations like feathered wingtips are often modeled as acoustic arrays where spatial spacing, sweep, out-of-plane displacement, and incidence vary across elements. While these features suggest bio-inspired noise control through phase interference, directivity changes can also arise from incoherent source-strength redistribution, dipole-axis rotation, decoherence, or radiation filtering. Directivity changes alone therefore do not identify the active mechanism.

This paper addresses this ambiguity by formulating segmented-wingtip directivity control as a radiating-covariance problem. A segmented aeroacoustic surface can control far-field directivity only through the source-covariance modes that project onto the acoustic radiation operator. Phase steering is available only when radiating off-diagonal covariance survives; otherwise, geometry controls directivity through source weighting, local dipole rotation, and incoherent radiation weighting.

The primary contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A positive-semidefinite radiating-covariance identity that decomposes far-field pressure spectra into independently radiating source-covariance eigenmodes.
    \item Formal limiting-case theorems for PSD admissibility, diagonal no-phase behavior, rank-one coherent-array behavior, off-diagonal interference bounds, compact phase weakness, and decoherence limits.
    \item Radiation-weighted mechanism indices, including raw coherence availability $\mathcal P_0$, radiating phase-steerability $\mathcal P_R$, radiating covariance fraction $\mathcal R$, and spherical-harmonic directivity control dimension $D_{\mathrm{control}}$.
    \item Exact computational validation and canonical benchmarks showing recovery of incoherent source-mixer and coherent phased-array limits.
    \item A partial-coherence mechanism phase diagram and a reanalysis of the existing 50-target feathered-wingtip campaign through the radiating-covariance metrics.
    \item FWA-Bench-0, an open analytical benchmark package containing exact array, diagonal, partial-coherence, and feathered-wingtip cases.
\end{enumerate}

We emphasize that this Version 1 paper is theoretical and computational. It does not use CFD or wind-tunnel data, does not claim absolute broadband noise reduction, and does not claim that feathered wingtips are automatically phase-steering arrays. Its purpose is to make the mechanism-selection theory internally consistent, exactly benchmarked, and reproducible under declared linear far-field stochastic-source assumptions.

\section{Literature Review}

This work builds upon three established research areas: analytical aeroacoustic loading-noise theory, bio-inspired edge-noise control, and acoustic-array optimization. Classical aerodynamic sound theories, including Curle's loading noise extension of Lighthill's analogy~\cite{Curle1955} and Farassat's formulations~\cite{Farassat2007}, describe acoustic radiation from unsteady surface forces. Trailing-edge and self-noise models~\cite{Amiet1976,Howe1978,Brooks1989} establish how turbulent boundary-layer scattering radiates broadband noise. Furthermore, prior studies on serrated edges demonstrate that edge geometry alters directivity and that destructive interference can reduce far-field noise at specific frequencies and angles~\cite{Lyu2016SerratedTE}.

Rather than proposing a new acoustic analogy, we implement a closure-based loading-noise surrogate to evaluate relative directivity changes. Because feathered geometries consist of spatially separated, differently oriented source regions, the far-field radiation depends on both local source strength and spatial cross-coherence. While ad-hoc cosine-summed cross-term models can yield non-physical negative power spectral densities (PSDs) under inconsistent coherence closures, we employ a Hermitian positive-semidefinite matrix construction. This formulation guarantees non-negative spectra across all frequencies and observer directions, enabling mathematically consistent comparisons between the zero-coherence, no-delay, and full-coherence limits.

Finally, we utilize thin-airfoil lift and drag proxies as relative screening metrics. These proxies estimate the aerodynamic penalties associated with directivity-optimized mutations without asserting absolute aerodynamic performance.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Radiating-covariance operator identity}

The central object in this paper is the observer-side quadratic form:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega),
\label{eq:radiating_covariance_identity}
\end{equation}
where $\mat{C}_q$ is the source cross-spectral covariance matrix and $\vect{a}$ is the radiation vector collecting geometry, propagation phase, transfer, quadrature weight, chord scaling, and local dipole projection. If
\begin{equation}
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    =
    \mat{U}(\omega)\mat{\Lambda}(\omega)\mat{U}^{H}(\omega)
    =
    \sum_{r=1}^{N_s}
    \lambda_r(\omega)
    \vect{u}_r(\omega)\vect{u}_r^{H}(\omega),
\label{eq:covariance_eigendecomposition}
\end{equation}
then Eq.~\ref{eq:radiating_covariance_identity} becomes:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_{r=1}^{N_s}
    \lambda_r(\omega)
    \left|
        \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
        \vect{u}_r(\omega)
    \right|^2.
\label{eq:modal_radiation_identity}
\end{equation}
This modal radiation identity is the theoretical core of the paper. The eigenvalue $\lambda_r$ measures the strength of source covariance mode $r$, the eigenvector $\vect{u}_r$ describes its coherent spatial structure, and $\left|\vect{a}^{H}\vect{u}_r\right|^2$ measures how efficiently that source mode radiates in observer direction $\vect{n}$. Directivity control is therefore governed by the interaction between geometry and radiating source-covariance modes, not by geometry alone.

For an observer set $\{\vect{n}_o,w_o\}$, define the integrated radiation operator:
\begin{equation}
    \mat{W}(\omega)
    =
    \sum_o
    w_o
    \vect{a}_o(\omega)\vect{a}_o^{H}(\omega),
\label{eq:radiation_operator}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{a}_o=\vect{a}(\vect{n}_o,\omega)$. The raw off-diagonal coherence availability is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal P_0(\omega)
    =
    \frac{
    \left\|
        \mat{C}_q-\operatorname{diag}(\mat{C}_q)
    \right\|_F
    }{
    \left\|
        \mat{C}_q
    \right\|_F
    }.
\label{eq:p0}
\end{equation}
The radiation-weighted phase-steerability index is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal P_R(\omega)
    =
    \frac{
    \left\|
        \mat{W}^{1/2}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\mat{W}^{1/2}
    \right\|_F
    }{
    \left\|
        \mat{W}^{1/2}\mat{C}_{q}\mat{W}^{1/2}
    \right\|_F
    },
    \qquad
    \mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}
    =
    \mat{C}_q-\operatorname{diag}(\mat{C}_q).
\label{eq:pr}
\end{equation}
Unlike $\mathcal P_0$, $\mathcal P_R$ penalizes coherent covariance that exists in source space but projects weakly onto the far-field radiation operator. With $\mat{W}$ normalized to unit mean eigenvalue, we also define the radiating covariance fraction:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal R(\omega)
    =
    \frac{
        \operatorname{tr}(\mat{W}\mat{C}_q)
    }{
        \operatorname{tr}(\mat{C}_q)
    }.
\label{eq:radiating_fraction}
\end{equation}

Finally, directivity is projected onto spherical harmonics:
\begin{equation}
    L(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \sum_{\ell=0}^{L_{\max}}
    \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}
    b_{\ell m}(\omega)
    Y_{\ell m}(\vect{n}),
\label{eq:spherical_directivity}
\end{equation}
and the geometry-to-directivity controllability Jacobian is:
\begin{equation}
    J_{\ell m,j}
    =
    \frac{\partial b_{\ell m}}{\partial \mu_j}.
\label{eq:controllability_jacobian}
\end{equation}
The directivity control dimension is the numerical rank:
\begin{equation}
    D_{\mathrm{control}}(\omega)
    =
    \#\left\{
        \sigma_i/\sigma_1>\epsilon
    \right\},
\label{eq:control_dimension}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i$ are singular values of $J$ and $\epsilon$ is a declared tolerance. A target projection score
\begin{equation}
    Q_T
    =
    \frac{\|P_JT\|_2^2}{\|T\|_2^2}
\label{eq:target_projection}
\end{equation}
measures how much of a target directivity lies in the locally controllable subspace.

\subsection{Physical assumptions and geometry representation}

We assume linear, subsonic, far-field acoustics in a uniform medium with density $\rho_0$, sound speed $c_0$, free-stream speed $U_\infty$, and Mach number
\begin{equation}
    M_\infty = \frac{U_\infty}{c_0}.
\label{eq:mach}
\end{equation}
The observer direction is defined by the unit vector:
\begin{equation}
    \vect{n} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    n_x & n_y & n_z
    \end{bmatrix}^{T},
    \qquad
    \|\vect{n}\| = 1.
\end{equation}
Feathers are modeled as rigid geometries represented by distributed stochastic loading-noise source lines. Structural vibrations, compliance, and porosity are neglected.

For feather $i$, the coordinate $\eta \in [0, 1]$ parameterizes the source point:
\begin{equation}
    \vect{r}_i(\eta;\vect{\mu}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    x_i(\eta)\\
    y_i(\eta)\\
    z_i(\eta)
    \end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:source_position}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{\mu}$ is the design vector. The arc length element is $\dd s_i = J_i(\eta)\dd\eta$, with Jacobian:
\begin{equation}
    J_i(\eta)=
    \left\|
        \frac{\partial\vect{r}_i}{\partial\eta}
    \right\|.
\end{equation}
We estimate $J_i$ via finite differences and apply trapezoidal quadrature. 

Root vertical translation, tip sweep, and tip vertical curvature across the feathers $s \in [0, 1]$ are parameterized by quadratic profiles:
\begin{equation}
    g(s)=g_1L_1(s)+g_mL_m(s)+g_7L_7(s),
\end{equation}
where $g_1$, $g_m$, and $g_7$ represent control points for the first, middle, and seventh feathers. The seven feather incidence values $\alpha_i$ are represented in an orthonormal cosine basis with $N_b = N = 7$ modes (one per feather, forming a full-rank square system):
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_i =
    \sum_{m=0}^{N_b-1}
    B_{im}a_m,
\label{eq:incidence_basis}
\end{equation}
where $a_m$ are incidence coefficients and $B_{im}$ is the $N \times N_b$ basis matrix.

\subsection{Acoustic model hierarchy}

We formulate the aeroacoustic model in three levels to isolate physical assumptions of compactness, spatial distribution, and coherence.

\subsubsection{Level 1: compact coherent feather array}
Each feather is treated as a compact source at $\vect{r}_i$. For observer direction $\vect{n}$ and frequency $\omega$, the far-field pressure and deterministic power are:
\begin{equation}
    p_1(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    A_i(\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
    \right],
    \qquad
    k=\frac{\omega}{c_0},
\label{eq:compact_array_pressure}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    |p_1(\vect{n},\omega)|^2
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left|
        \sum_{i=1}^{N}
        A_i q_i
        \exp\left[
            \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
        \right]
    \right|^2,
\end{equation}
where $q_i(\omega)$ is the complex source amplitude, and $A_i$ accounts for local loading and transfer.

\subsubsection{Level 2: deterministic distributed source lines}
To account for spatial distribution, we integrate along each feather's source line:
\begin{equation}
    p_2(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\eta,\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i(\eta)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)\dd\eta,
\label{eq:distributed_pressure}
\end{equation}
where $H_i$ collects loading direction and propagation transfer. 

\subsubsection{Level 3: stochastic cross-spectral model}
To represent turbulent loading noise, we define the source cross-spectrum:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        q_i(\eta,\omega)q_j^*(\eta',\omega)
    \right].
\label{eq:continuous_cross_spectrum}
\end{equation}
The far-field pressure power spectral density (PSD) becomes:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:continuous_psd}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    &=
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \sum_{j=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    H_j^*(\eta',\vect{n},\omega)
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    \nonumber\\
    &\quad\times
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot
        \left(
            \vect{r}_i(\eta)-\vect{r}_j(\eta')
        \right)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)J_j(\eta')\dd\eta\dd\eta'.
\end{align}

Discretizing the quadrature yields the quadratic form:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega),
\label{eq:discrete_psd}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{a}$ aggregates transfer factors, loading orientation, phase, and weights. The source cross-spectral matrix is:
\begin{equation}
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        \vect{q}(\omega)\vect{q}^H(\omega)
    \right].
\end{equation}
By constructing $\mat{C}_q$ as Hermitian positive-semidefinite ($\mat{C}_q \succeq 0$), we mathematically guarantee non-negative pressure spectra ($S_{pp} \geq 0$) for all observer angles and frequencies.

\subsection{Closure models and nondimensional regime}

The source cross-spectral matrix is closed using:
\begin{equation}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega)
    =
    \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega),
\label{eq:source_cross_spectrum}
\end{equation}
where the source autospectrum is:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_m(\omega)
    =
    C_\Phi\rho_0^2U_\infty^5c_m^2
    F_\chi(\chi_m)
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m),
\label{eq:source_autospectrum}
\end{equation}
with $C_\Phi$ a scalar calibration constant (not to be confused with the matrix $\mat{C}_q$), and:
\begin{equation}
    \chi_m=\frac{\omega c_m \mathit{St}_0}{U_c},
    \qquad
    U_c=\beta U_\infty,
\end{equation}
where $\mathit{St}_0$ is a fixed Strouhal scale parameter that maps chord-based frequency to the spectral shape function $F_\chi$ (see Table~\ref{tab:appendix_parameters} for its numerical value).
\begin{equation}
    F_\chi(\chi)=
    \frac{\chi^2}{(1+\chi^2)^{7/3}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    1+a_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}})^2.
\end{equation}

The spatial coherence magnitude and incidence-dependent phase delay are modeled as:
\begin{equation}
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    =
    \exp\left[
        -\frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}}
        \sqrt{
            \left(\frac{\Delta x_{mn}}{a_x}\right)^2
            +
            \left(\frac{\Delta y_{mn}}{a_y}\right)^2
            +
            \left(\frac{\Delta z_{mn}}{a_z}\right)^2
        }
    \right],
\label{eq:coherence_magnitude}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}}),
\label{eq:incidence_delay}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega)
    =
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    \exp\left[
        \ii\omega
        \left(
            \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)-\tau_\alpha(\alpha_n)
        \right)
    \right].
\label{eq:complex_coherence}
\end{equation}
The baseline closures ($a_x=a_y=0.9\,\mathrm{m}$, $a_z=0.3\,\mathrm{m}$, $f_{\mathrm{ref}}=1500\,\mathrm{Hz}$, $\beta=0.85$, and $b_\alpha=5.0\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{s\,rad^{-1}}$) ensure neighboring feathers remain partially coherent at low-to-mid frequencies while maintaining active phase shifts.

We analyze the system using the center-wing chord $c_{\mathrm{ref}} = 0.30\,\mathrm{m}$ to define nondimensional groups:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\vect{r}}=\frac{\vect{r}}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}},
    \qquad
    \kappa = kc_{\mathrm{ref}} = \frac{2\pi f c_{\mathrm{ref}}}{c_0},
    \qquad
    \tilde{a}_j = \frac{a_j}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}},
    \qquad
    \Pi_\alpha = \frac{U_\infty b_\alpha}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}}.
\label{eq:nondim_groups}
\end{equation}
The coherence decay is governed by the dimensionless separation:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{d}_{mn}
    =
    \sqrt{
        \left(\frac{\Delta x_{mn}}{a_x}\right)^2
        +
        \left(\frac{\Delta y_{mn}}{a_y}\right)^2
        +
        \left(\frac{\Delta z_{mn}}{a_z}\right)^2
    },
\label{eq:nondim_coherence_distance}
\end{equation}
and the delay phase group:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta_{mn}
    =
    \omega b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_n).
\label{eq:delay_phase_group}
\end{equation}

The transfer weight for source point $m$ is:
\begin{equation}
    a_m(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    c_m
    \left(
        \vect{l}_m\cdot\vect{n}
    \right)
    w_m
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m
    \right],
\label{eq:transfer_weight}
\end{equation}
with kernel:
\begin{equation}
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{\omega/c_0}{(1-M_\infty n_x)^2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Surrogate validation, target fit, and aerodynamic proxies}

We validate optimized geometries using a separate boundary-layer self-noise surrogate simulator. This simulator uses BPM/TNO-inspired spectral closures (trailing-edge, separation, bluntness, and tip-vortex contributions) rather than the optimizer's compact loading-noise closures. The local aerodynamic state relies on a thin-airfoil lift proxy with soft stall:
\begin{equation}
    C_l =
    2\pi\alpha
    \left[
        1+
        \left(
            \frac{|\alpha|}{\alpha_{\mathrm{stall}}}
        \right)^8
    \right]^{-1},
\label{eq:lift_proxy}
\end{equation}
and drag coefficient $C_d = C_{d0}+k_dC_l^2$.

Target directivity patterns $T_o$ are generated with $N_\ell = 1, 2,$ or $3$ smooth lobes:
\begin{equation}
    T_o =
    \sum_{\ell=1}^{N_\ell}
    A_\ell
    \left[
        \frac{1+\vect{n}_o\cdot\vect{c}_\ell}{2}
    \right]^{p_\ell}.
\label{eq:target_generation}
\end{equation}
The optimizer minimizes the mean-squared error of peak-normalized directivities:
\begin{equation}
    J_{\mathrm{fit}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left(
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}
        -
        \hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right)^2.
\label{eq:fit_objective}
\end{equation}
We report target-fit and direction errors:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:fit_metrics}
    E_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
    &=
    \left[
        \frac{1}{N_o}
        \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
        \left(
            \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
        \right)^2
    \right]^{1/2},\\
    E_{\mathrm{MAE}}
    &=
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left|
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right|,
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{peak}}
    =
    \cos^{-1}
    \left(
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{target\,peak}}
        \cdot
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{predicted\,peak}}
    \right).
\end{equation}

Aerodynamic performance is tracked using integrated lift and drag proxies:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:aero_proxies}
    L^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{l,m}c_m\Delta s_m,\\
    D^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{d,m}c_m\Delta s_m,
\end{align}
and the ratio $(L/D)^* = L^*/D^*$. Baseline-relative retention ratios $R_L$, $R_D$, and $R_{L/D}$ measure the aerodynamic impact of structural mutations.

\subsection{Mechanism ablation and optimization campaign}

We isolate acoustic mechanisms by optimizing each target under four distinct configurations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Incidence only} (\texttt{incidence}): Spatial parameters are fixed; only the 7 incidence coefficients are optimized.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, zero coherence} (\texttt{zero\_coherence}): The full 18-variable vector is optimized, but $\Gamma_{mn} = \delta_{mn}$, disabling coherent phase-steering.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, no delay} (\texttt{no\_delay}): The full 18-variable vector is optimized, but $\tau_\alpha = 0$, disabling incidence-delay phase shifts.
    \item \textbf{Full model} (\texttt{full}): The full 18-variable design space is optimized under the complete model.
\end{enumerate}

The genetic optimizer searches the 18 design variables using a differential-evolution population of size:
\begin{equation}
    P=N_pN_\mu,
\label{eq:population_size}
\end{equation}
where $N_\mu = 18$ and $N_p = 9$ (the population multiplier). Candidates are penalized for physical source-line intersections. We employ a two-stage evaluation: candidates are screened on a coarse grid (observer stride 2, frequency stride 2, $n_\eta = 5$), and the top fraction ($18\%$, with a minimum of 6 candidates) is evaluated at the full-resolution observer and frequency grid. The mutation factor is drawn uniformly from $[0.5, 1.0]$ per generation, the crossover probability is $0.7$, and early stopping is triggered after $20$ consecutive generations without improvement exceeding $0.015\,\mathrm{dB}^2$.

To accelerate the genetic search, the PyTorch acoustic path refactors the CSD matrix projection to scale transfer weights $\vect{a}_m$ directly by $\sqrt{\Phi_m}$. This changes the memory footprint from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$, and maps tensor contractions to hardware matrix units (AMX/MPS). For the \texttt{zero\_coherence} ablation, a specialized fast path skips distance and matrix calculations entirely, evaluating far-field pressure power as $S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) = \sum_{m} |a_{m}|^2$.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

We perform a paired repeated-target analysis across 50 random target seeds. Pairwise contrasts $d_s^{(A,B)} = y_s^{(A)} - y_s^{(B)}$ for response variables $y$ (validated RMSE, drag ratio, and $L/D$ retention) are evaluated using two-sided paired Student-$t$ tests. Family-wise error rates are controlled using Holm-adjusted $p$-values.

\section{Mathematical and Numerical Validation}

To ensure the physical validity, numerical robustness, and interpretability of the radiating-covariance framework, this section combines formal limiting-case propositions with exact computational validation. The propositions establish PSD admissibility, Hermitian symmetry, covariance eigenmode radiation, diagonal no-phase behavior, rank-one coherent-array recovery, off-diagonal interference bounds, compact/decoherence limits, and directivity controllability limits. The subsequent numerical checks verify grid convergence, calibration-shape invariance, and the phase-mechanism interpretation of the optimization campaign.

\subsection{Proposition 1 --- Power Spectral Density Non-negativity}

Let the source cross-spectral matrix $\mat{C}_q(\omega) \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times N}$ be positive-semidefinite (PSD), denoted as $\mat{C}_q(\omega) \succeq 0$. We show that the far-field pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) \ge 0$ for all frequencies $f = \omega / (2\pi) \ge 0$ and observer directions $\vect{n}$. This proof relies on the physical assumptions that the observer distance satisfies $R > 0$, the reference frequency satisfies $f_{\mathrm{ref}} > 0$, the coherence decay parameters satisfy $a_x, a_y, a_z > 0$, the source autospectra satisfy $\Phi_m(\omega) \ge 0$, and the frequency parameter satisfies $f \ge 0$.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Recall the discretized far-field pressure PSD given by Eq.~\ref{eq:discrete_psd}:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega).
\end{equation*}
By definition of a positive-semidefinite matrix, for any complex vector $\vect{z} \in \mathbb{C}^N$, the quadratic form satisfies:
\begin{equation*}
    \vect{z}^H \mat{C}_q(\omega) \vect{z} \ge 0.
\end{equation*}
Since $\vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega) \in \mathbb{C}^N$ is a well-defined complex transfer weight vector, and since the propagation distance factor $R > 0$ yields a positive real denominator $(4\pi R)^2 > 0$, substituting $\vect{z} = \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega)$ directly yields:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) \ge 0.
\end{equation*}
This guarantees that the predicted acoustic power remains non-negative under any observer angle and frequency, preventing non-physical negative energy states. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\vspace{1em}
We now prove that each of the three implemented coherence models yields $\mat{C}_q(\omega) \succeq 0$. The source cross-spectral matrix is closed via Eq.~\ref{eq:source_cross_spectrum} as:
\begin{equation*}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega) = \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)} \Gamma_{mn}(\omega).
\end{equation*}
Let $\vect{v} = [\sqrt{\Phi_1}, \dots, \sqrt{\Phi_N}]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be a real-valued vector of source amplitude terms (since $\Phi_i \ge 0$). We define the rank-1 outer product matrix $\mat{S} = \vect{v}\vect{v}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$. For any complex vector $\vect{z} \in \mathbb{C}^N$:
\begin{equation*}
    \vect{z}^H \mat{S} \vect{z}
    =
    \vect{z}^H (\vect{v}\vect{v}^T) \vect{z}
    =
    (\vect{z}^H \vect{v})(\vect{v}^T \vect{z})
    =
    \left| \vect{z}^H \vect{v} \right|^2 \ge 0.
\end{equation*}
Hence, $\mat{S} \succeq 0$. The source cross-spectral matrix can be written as the Hadamard (entrywise) product:
\begin{equation*}
    \mat{C}_q = \mat{S} \circ \mat{\Gamma},
\end{equation*}
where $\mat{\Gamma} \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times N}$ is the spatial coherence matrix containing $\Gamma_{mn}$. By the Schur Product Theorem, the Hadamard product of two positive-semidefinite matrices is also positive-semidefinite. Therefore, to show $\mat{C}_q \succeq 0$, it is sufficient to prove that the spatial coherence matrix $\mat{\Gamma} \succeq 0$ for each implemented model.

\paragraph{1. Zero Coherence Model.}
Under the \texttt{zero\_coherence} limit, the coherence elements are:
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma_{mn} = \delta_{mn},
\end{equation*}
which corresponds to the identity matrix $\mat{\Gamma} = \mat{I}$. Because all eigenvalues of the identity matrix are unity ($1 > 0$), $\mat{I}$ is positive-definite, and hence $\mat{\Gamma} \succeq 0$.

\paragraph{2. Full Coherence Model.}
In the completely coherent limit, the complex coherence function is:
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega) = \exp\left[ \ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) \right) \right].
\end{equation*}
Defining the complex vector $\vect{u} \in \mathbb{C}^N$ with elements $u_m = \exp\left[\ii \omega \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)\right]$, the coherence matrix can be expressed as the rank-1 outer product $\mat{\Gamma} = \vect{u}\vect{u}^H$. For any $\vect{z} \in \mathbb{C}^N$:
\begin{equation*}
    \vect{z}^H \mat{\Gamma} \vect{z}
    =
    \vect{z}^H (\vect{u}\vect{u}^H) \vect{z}
    =
    \left| \vect{z}^H \vect{u} \right|^2 \ge 0.
\end{equation*}
Thus, $\mat{\Gamma} \succeq 0$.

\paragraph{3. Exponential Coherence Model.}
For the partially coherent case, the coherence matrix incorporates spatial decay:
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega) = \exp\left[ - \tilde{d}_{mn} \frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right] \exp\left[ \ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) \right) \right].
\end{equation*}
Let $\mat{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be the real spatial correlation matrix with elements:
\begin{equation*}
    D_{mn} = \exp\left[ - \tilde{d}_{mn} \frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right],
\end{equation*}
where $\tilde{d}_{mn}$ is the dimensionless distance metric given by Eq.~\ref{eq:nondim_coherence_distance}. The matrix $\mat{\Gamma}$ can be written as the Hadamard product of $\mat{D}$ and the full coherence rank-1 outer product:
\begin{equation*}
    \mat{\Gamma} = \mat{D} \circ (\vect{u}\vect{u}^H).
\end{equation*}
By the Schur Product Theorem, since $\vect{u}\vect{u}^H \succeq 0$, it suffices to show $\mat{D} \succeq 0$.
The elements $D_{mn}$ are evaluated as:
\begin{equation*}
    D_{mn} = \exp\left[ - c \left\| \vect{x}'_m - \vect{x}'_n \right\|_2 \right],
\end{equation*}
where $c = f/f_{\mathrm{ref}} \ge 0$ is a non-negative scalar, and the scaled coordinate vector of source point $m$ is $\vect{x}'_m = [x_m/a_x, y_m/a_y, z_m/a_z]^T \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (which is well-defined under $a_x, a_y, a_z > 0$). According to Schoenberg's theorem, the radial basis function $k(\vect{x}', \vect{y}') = \exp\left( - c \|\vect{x}' - \vect{y}'\|_2^\alpha \right)$ is positive-semidefinite in any Euclidean space for exponent $0 < \alpha \le 2$. The implemented coherence model corresponds to $\alpha=1$, hence $\mat{D}\succeq0$ for any frequency $f \ge 0$ (with $c=f/f_{\mathrm{ref}}\ge0$). At the zero-frequency limit ($f = 0 \implies c=0$), the spatial correlation matrix reduces to $D_{mn} = 1$ (the all-ones matrix), which is positive-semidefinite.
Therefore, by applying the Schur Product Theorem twice, we obtain $\mat{\Gamma} \succeq 0$, and consequently $\mat{C}_q(\omega) \succeq 0$ for all models. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 2 --- Hermitian Symmetry}

We show that the source cross-spectral matrix $\mat{C}_q(\omega)$ satisfies Hermitian symmetry:
\begin{equation*}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega) = \overline{C_{q,nm}(\omega)},
\end{equation*}
for all indices $m, n \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ and all implemented coherence modes.

\paragraph{Proof.}
By definition:
\begin{equation*}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega) = \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)} \Gamma_{mn}(\omega).
\end{equation*}
Taking the complex conjugate of the entry at $(n,m)$ yields:
\begin{equation*}
    \overline{C_{q,nm}(\omega)}
    =
    \overline{\sqrt{\Phi_n(\omega)\Phi_m(\omega)} \Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}
    =
    \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)} \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)},
\end{equation*}
because the source autospectra $\Phi_i(\omega) \ge 0$ are real-valued. Thus, $C_{q,mn}(\omega) = \overline{C_{q,nm}(\omega)}$ is satisfied if and only if:
\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega) = \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}.
\end{equation*}
We verify this relation for each coherence model:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zero Coherence:} Since $\Gamma_{mn} = \delta_{mn}$ is real-valued and symmetric ($\delta_{mn} = \delta_{nm}$), we have:
    \begin{equation*}
        \overline{\Gamma_{nm}} = \overline{\delta_{nm}} = \delta_{nm} = \delta_{mn} = \Gamma_{mn}.
    \end{equation*}
    \item \textbf{Full Coherence:}
    \begin{equation*}
        \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}
        =
        \overline{\exp\left[ \ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) \right) \right]}
        =
        \exp\left[ -\ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) \right) \right]
        =
        \Gamma_{mn}(\omega).
    \end{equation*}
    \item \textbf{Exponential Coherence:}
    \begin{equation*}
        \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}
        =
        \overline{\exp\left[ - \tilde{d}_{nm} \frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right] \exp\left[ \ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) \right) \right]}.
    \end{equation*}
    Because $\tilde{d}_{nm} = \tilde{d}_{mn}$ is the symmetric Euclidean distance, the amplitude decay factor is real and symmetric. Evaluating the conjugate gives:
    \begin{align*}
        \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}
        &=
        \exp\left[ - \tilde{d}_{mn} \frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right] \exp\left[ -\ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) \right) \right]
        \nonumber\\
        &=
        \exp\left[ - \tilde{d}_{mn} \frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}} \right] \exp\left[ \ii \omega \left( \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m) - \tau_\alpha(\alpha_n) \right) \right]
        =
        \Gamma_{mn}(\omega).
    \end{align*}
\end{enumerate}
In all cases, the coherence function satisfies $\Gamma_{mn}(\omega) = \overline{\Gamma_{nm}(\omega)}$, confirming that the cross-spectral matrix $\mat{C}_q(\omega)$ is Hermitian. A direct physical consequence of this Hermitian symmetry is that the quadratic form $\vect{a}^H \mat{C}_q \vect{a}$ is guaranteed to be real-valued ($\vect{a}^H \mat{C}_q \vect{a} \in \mathbb{R}$), ensuring that the predicted far-field pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:discrete_psd} is always a real, physically interpretable quantity. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 3 --- Radiating Eigenmode Decomposition}

If $\mat{C}_q\succeq0$ and $\mat{C}_q=\sum_r\lambda_r\vect{u}_r\vect{u}_r^H$, then the far-field spectrum is a sum of independently radiating source-covariance modes:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_r
    \lambda_r
    \left|
        \vect{a}^H(\vect{n},\omega)\vect{u}_r
    \right|^2.
\end{equation*}
This follows by substituting the eigendecomposition of $\mat{C}_q$ into Eq.~\ref{eq:radiating_covariance_identity}. Each term is non-negative because $\lambda_r\ge0$ and the projection magnitude is squared. This result is stronger than PSD admissibility: it gives a physical basis for mechanism selection because only covariance eigenmodes with nonzero radiation projection can shape far-field directivity. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 4 --- Diagonal-Covariance No-Phase Limit}

If $\mat{C}_q=\operatorname{diag}(\Phi_1,\ldots,\Phi_N)$, then:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_m
    \Phi_m |a_m|^2.
\end{equation*}
Since $a_m=K_Hc_m(\vect{l}_m\cdot\vect{n})w_m\exp(\ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m)$, the path phase cancels inside $|a_m|^2$. Thus, in the diagonal covariance limit, inter-source path-length phase cannot create constructive or destructive interference. Geometry can still change directivity through source strength, chord weighting, quadrature length, transfer magnitude, and local dipole projection, but not through inter-feather phase steering. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 5 --- Rank-One Coherent Array Limit}

If $\mat{C}_q=\vect{q}\vect{q}^{H}$, then:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left|
        \vect{a}^{H}\vect{q}
    \right|^2.
\end{equation*}
For equal source amplitudes, equal transfer magnitudes, fixed dipole axes, and a uniform linear spacing $s$, this reduces to the classical array-factor form:
\begin{equation*}
    S_{pp}(\theta)
    \propto
    \left|
        \sum_{m=0}^{N-1}
        \exp\left(\ii mks\sin\theta\right)
    \right|^2.
\end{equation*}
The model therefore recovers a phase-dominant acoustic array exactly when the source covariance permits coherent rank-one radiation. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 6 --- Off-Diagonal Interference Bound}

Let $\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}=\mat{C}_q-\operatorname{diag}(\mat{C}_q)$. The interference contribution is:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta S_{\mathrm{int}}
    =
    \vect{a}^{H}\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\vect{a}.
\end{equation*}
By submultiplicativity and $\|\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\|_2\le\|\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\|_F$:
\begin{equation*}
    |\Delta S_{\mathrm{int}}|
    \le
    \|\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\|_2\|\vect{a}\|_2^2
    \le
    \|\mat{C}_{\mathrm{off}}\|_F\|\vect{a}\|_2^2.
\end{equation*}
If the radiating off-diagonal covariance is small, then phase interference cannot dominate the far-field spectrum regardless of geometric phase opportunities. This motivates the radiation-weighted index $\mathcal P_R$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:pr}. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 7 --- Compact and Decoherence Limits}

For source spacing scale $s$ with $ks\ll1$,
\begin{equation*}
    \exp(\ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m)
    =
    1+\ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m+O((ks)^2),
\end{equation*}
so path-length phase variation is asymptotically weak. Directivity control in this compact limit is dominated by dipole projection and source weighting rather than path phase. Conversely, in the exponential coherence model, if $(f/f_{\mathrm{ref}})\tilde d_{mn}\rightarrow\infty$ for $m\neq n$, then $\Gamma_{mn}\rightarrow0$ and $\mat{C}_q\rightarrow\operatorname{diag}(\mat{C}_q)$. High acoustic noncompactness does not by itself imply phase steering; usable phase control requires both geometric phase separation and surviving off-diagonal source covariance. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Proposition 8 --- Directivity Controllability Limit}

Let $J_{\ell m,j}=\partial b_{\ell m}/\partial\mu_j$ be the local geometry-to-spherical-harmonic Jacobian in Eq.~\ref{eq:controllability_jacobian}. A target directivity can be changed locally only through the column space of $J$. Therefore, the number of locally controllable directivity degrees of freedom is bounded by $D_{\mathrm{control}}=\operatorname{rank}_\epsilon(J)$ and cannot exceed the number of active design variables. This shows why a feathered wingtip cannot fit arbitrary directivity patterns: it can fit only the target components that project onto its controllable radiation subspace. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\subsection{Numerical Verification 9 --- Grid Convergence}

We numerically verify that the far-field pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}^{(n_\eta)}(f, \vect{n})$ computed with spanwise discretization size $n_\eta$ converges to a unique limit as $n_\eta \to \infty$ for the evaluated configurations:
\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{n_\eta \to \infty} S_{pp}^{(n_\eta)}(f,\vect{n}) = S_{pp}^{(\infty)}(f,\vect{n}).
\end{equation*}
Due to local coordinate variations and phase cancellations, convergence rates are evaluated empirically across the parameter space.

\paragraph{Numerical Verification.}
We evaluate the far-field spectrum for the default wingtip geometry using the baseline exponential coherence model. The test case uses an overhead observer ($\vect{n} = [0, 0, 1]^T$) and an acoustic frequency $f = 1000$ Hz. \begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\subsection{Empirical Verification 10 --- Calibration Invariance of Directivity Shape}

We verify that a scalar level calibration leaves centered or peak-normalized directivity shapes invariant in decibels. This mathematical property justifies optimizing relative directivity shapes using the fast compact-source formulation, even when absolute levels differ.

\paragraph{Verification.}
Due to chord-integrated source strength approximations in the compact-source formulation (Level 3 model) compared to the distributed surface representation in the distributed low-order self-noise surrogate simulation, the uncalibrated theoretical model underpredicts the absolute acoustic pressure levels. We model this mismatch as a uniform scalar offset. Let $S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)$ be the uncalibrated far-field pressure power spectral density (PSD), and let:
\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{S}_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) = C_{\mathrm{cal}} S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
\end{equation*}
be the calibrated PSD, where $C_{\mathrm{cal}} = 10^{\Delta L_{\mathrm{offset}} / 10} > 0$ is a frequency-independent and observer-independent scalar calibration factor. Taking the decibel level yields:
\begin{equation*}
    10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{\tilde{S}_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)}{p_{\mathrm{ref}}^2} \right) = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)}{p_{\mathrm{ref}}^2} \right) + \Delta L_{\mathrm{offset}}.
\end{equation*}
This linear relationship implies that scalar calibration shifts the entire predicted far-field spectrum uniformly in decibels, leaving the relative directivity shapes and lobe angles unchanged. To verify this shape invariance numerically, we evaluate validation metrics (Bias, Mean Absolute Error [MAE], and Root-Mean-Square Error [RMSE]) before and after scalar calibration.

We check two datasets: first, the representative dense validation case (target seed 19, grid size $n_\eta = 48$); second, the study-wide dataset aggregated across all 50 optimized targets ($29{,}400$ total points, campaign grid size $n_\eta = 12$). The results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:calibration_separability}.

\subsection{Empirical Analysis 11 --- Phase Mechanism}

We analyze the phase mechanism using a four-step conditional reasoning framework to reconcile the local importance of phase interference with the optimizer's ability to achieve equivalent performance without it.

\paragraph{Step 1: Equivalent optimization performance.}
First, the optimization campaign shows that the validated target-fit RMSE is statistically equivalent among the three full-geometry configurations:
\begin{align*}
    \text{Validated RMSE (\texttt{full})} &= 9.157 \pm 2.404\,\mathrm{dB}, \\
    \text{Validated RMSE (\texttt{no\_delay})} &= 9.109 \pm 2.578\,\mathrm{dB}, \\
    \text{Validated RMSE (\texttt{zero\_coherence})} &= 9.164 \pm 2.620\,\mathrm{dB}.
\end{align*}
To test this equivalence-fit performance rigorously, we perform a Two One-Sided Test (TOST) with an equivalence margin of $\delta = 0.25\,\mathrm{dB}$ across the 50 targets. The paired difference between the \texttt{full} and \texttt{no\_delay} configurations is $0.048\,\mathrm{dB}$ (95\% CI: $[-0.118, 0.214]\mathrm{dB}$, TOST $p = 0.0091$), and between the \texttt{full} and \texttt{zero\_coherence} configurations is $-0.006\,\mathrm{dB}$ (95\% CI: $[-0.199, 0.187]\mathrm{dB}$, TOST $p = 0.0072$). Both comparisons are statistically equivalent within the $\pm0.25\,\mathrm{dB}$ margin, showing that removing convective delay or cross-coherence does not degrade the optimizer's final target-matching capability.

\paragraph{Step 2: Direct ablation on fixed geometries.}
Second, we perform a direct ablation of phase coherence on the optimized geometries. For each of the 50 optimized \texttt{full} configurations, we evaluate the far-field SPL at $1000\,\mathrm{Hz}$ on a dense spherical observer grid using both the partially coherent model (\texttt{exponential}) and the uncorrelated model (\texttt{zero\_coherence}). This direct ablation of phase coherence yields a study-wide mean SPL shift of $\bar{\Delta L} = 0.907\,\mathrm{dB}$ but a large angular variation with a standard deviation of $\sigma_\Omega(\Delta L) = 5.883\,\mathrm{dB}$, corresponding to a centered shape RMSE of $5.863\,\mathrm{dB}$ and a Pearson correlation of only $\rho = 0.8422$.

\paragraph{Step 3: Importance of phase for fixed designs.}
Third, the direct ablation results demonstrate that removing phase coherence from a fixed optimized geometry significantly disrupts its local interference patterns and alters its directivity shape. Thus, phase interference is mathematically active and physically important for the acoustic response of any specific fixed wingtip design.

\paragraph{Step 4: Geometric redundancy in the design space.}
Fourth, because the optimization campaign achieves equivalent target-fit performance under zero coherence (Step 1), we conclude that the design space has sufficient geometric degrees of freedom (including chords, positions, and local dipole rotation via feather incidence) to allow the optimizer to re-synthesize target directivity patterns. Thus, the optimizer can compensate for the absence of phase steering through alternative geometric mechanisms, rendering inter-feather phase interference non-dominant for directivity control in this regime. $\hfill \blacksquare$

\section{Results}

Our 50-target optimization campaign (200 total runs across four configurations) shows that directivity control depends significantly on geometric configuration. We compare performance using final validated surrogate RMSE to avoid baseline biases across configurations.

\subsection{Radiating-covariance regime and campaign reanalysis}

The phase diagram in Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism_phase_diagram} confirms the expected limiting behavior: compact cases with $ks\ll1$ and highly decohered cases with $\ell_c/s\ll1$ have weak phase-ablation sensitivity, while phase-sensitive cases appear only when both geometric phase separation and radiating off-diagonal covariance are available. This validates the use of $\mathcal P_R$ as an availability metric for phase interference in controlled synthetic regimes.

The optimized feathered-wingtip campaign shows a subtler behavior. Across the 50 optimized full-model geometries, the mean raw coherence availability is high, the mean radiating phase-steerability is $\overline{\mathcal P_R}=0.811$, and the mean fixed-geometry phase-ablation RMSE is $7.93\,\mathrm{dB}$. The mean spherical-harmonic control dimension is $D_{\mathrm{control}}=8.58$ at the $\epsilon=10^{-2}$ threshold, and the mean target projection score is $\overline{Q_T}=0.856$. However, within this optimized subset, $\mathcal P_R$ explains only part of the variation in fixed-geometry phase-ablation effect ($R^2=0.214$), and the local target projection score alone does not predict final surrogate RMSE ($R^2=0.038$).

This does not contradict the phase diagram. Instead, it separates two questions: phase interference is active for fixed full-model geometries, but final target-fit performance after reoptimization can remain equivalent under zero coherence because the design space has alternative radiation and covariance directions available. The existing full/no-delay/zero-coherence equivalence should therefore be interpreted as geometric compensation and mechanism redundancy, not as evidence that phase terms are mathematically inactive.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Physical mechanisms of geometry-sensitive directivity control}

The primary result of our campaign is that full-geometry optimization achieves about $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ lower validated target-shape RMSE than incidence-only control, but the three full-geometry configurations (\texttt{full}, \texttt{no\_delay}, \texttt{zero\_coherence}) perform identically. 

To interpret this, we expand the far-field spectrum into diagonal (uncorrelated) and off-diagonal (coherent) terms:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left[
        \sum_m |a_m|^2 \Phi_m
        +
        \sum_{m\neq n}
        a_m^* a_n
        \sqrt{\Phi_m\Phi_n}
        \Gamma_{mn}
    \right].
\end{equation}
The diagonal terms depend on local source amplitudes, loading orientations, and chords, while the off-diagonal terms introduce spatial coherence and delay phases. The exact diagonal theorem shows that if $\Gamma_{mn}=\delta_{mn}$, then geometric phase cannot contribute. However, the campaign reanalysis shows that the optimized full-model geometries are not themselves diagonal-dominated: their fixed-geometry phase-ablation sensitivity is substantial. Therefore, the equivalence of \texttt{zero\_coherence} and the other configurations should be interpreted as design-space compensation after reoptimization, not as proof that the full-model geometries contain no active phase interference.
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}^{(0)}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_m
    \left|
        K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
        c_m
        (\vect{l}_m\cdot\vect{n})
        w_m
    \right|^2
    \Phi_m.
\end{equation}
In the zero-coherence reoptimized condition, directivity must be synthesized by spatial re-weighting, sweep-induced local dipole rotation ($\vect{l}_m\cdot\vect{n}$), chord/source weighting, and incoherent source redistribution. In the full condition, off-diagonal phase terms are active, but they are not uniquely required to reach the observed target-fit performance.

This is consistent with the direct ablation study: removing phase coherence from a fixed optimized full-model geometry changes the directivity shape, but allowing the optimizer to redesign the geometry under zero coherence restores comparable final target-fit performance. The mechanism is therefore not ``phase is absent,'' but ``phase is not the only available controllability channel.''

\subsection{Limitations and model bounds}

The theory--surrogate discrepancy increases significantly above $\kappa = kc_{\mathrm{ref}} \approx 11$ ($2\,\mathrm{kHz}$). This spectral discrepancy arises because the single-frequency calibration does not align the distinct spectral envelopes of the compact theoretical autospectrum and the multi-component, BPM/TNO-inspired surrogate. 

Therefore, the present framework serves as a relative screening and mechanism-isolation tool for low-to-mid-frequency directivity matching. It is not suitable for high-frequency absolute sound level predictions. Further calibration against high-fidelity experimental or computational datasets is required to establish physical closure parameters.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a radiating-covariance framework for segmented lifting-surface aeroacoustics. By using a Hermitian positive-semidefinite source covariance and explicitly decomposing it into radiating eigenmodes, the formulation guarantees physically valid pressure spectra while identifying which source-covariance modes are visible to the far field.

Exact computational validation confirms PSD admissibility, modal-radiation equivalence, diagonal no-phase behavior, rank-one coherent-array recovery, and the off-diagonal interference bound. The mechanism phase diagram shows that phase-sensitive behavior requires both geometric phase separation and radiating off-diagonal covariance. In the 50-target feathered-wingtip campaign, modifying full geometry achieved a $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ validated RMSE advantage over incidence-only control. However, the identical final performance of the \texttt{full}, \texttt{no\_delay}, and \texttt{zero\_coherence} models indicates mechanism redundancy after reoptimization: phase interference is active for fixed full-model geometries, but it is not uniquely required for target-shape matching in the tested regime. Future work should calibrate the closure constants against high-fidelity aerodynamic and acoustic data and extend the controllability analysis beyond local finite-difference Jacobians.

\paragraph{Differential Evolution Settings.}
The genetic optimization of the 18-variable wingtip geometry is governed by the following settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Population Size:} The population is set to $P = 162$, computed using a population multiplier of $N_p = 9$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:population_size}).
    \item \textbf{Mutation \& Crossover:} The mutation scale factor $F$ is drawn uniformly from $[0.5, 1.0]$ at each generation. The crossover probability is $C_r = 0.7$.
    \item \textbf{Two-Stage Evaluation:} To accelerate search, candidates are first screened on a coarse grid (observer stride 2, frequency stride 2, spanwise grid $n_\eta = 5$). The top $18\%$ of candidates (with a minimum of 6) are then evaluated at campaign resolution ($n_\eta = 12$, full observer and frequency grids).
    \item \textbf{Termination Criteria:} The optimization terminates when the objective function does not improve by more than $0.015\,\mathrm{dB}^2$ over 20 consecutive generations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation of the PSD Matrix}

The following pseudocode outlines the implementation used to evaluate the far-field pressure power spectral density across a set of observer directions and frequencies. It illustrates how the double-summation structure and local spanwise numerical integration are aggregated.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Evaluation of Positive-Semidefinite Array Far-Field Pressure Spectrum}
\label{alg:psd_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Observer direction set $\{\vect{n}\}$, Frequency set $\{\omega\}$, Discretized source points $m \in \{1 \dots M\}$ with coordinates $\{\vect{r}_m\}$, dipole loading directions $\{\vect{l}_m\}$, local chords $\{c_m\}$, local quadrature weights $\{w_m\}$, and local incidences $\{\alpha_m\}$
\Ensure Far-field acoustic pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}(\vect{n}, \omega)$
\For{each frequency $\omega$}
    \State Compute source autospectra vector $\vect{\Phi}(\omega) \in \mathbb{R}^M$ where $\Phi_m(\omega)$ is given by Eq.~\ref{eq:source_autospectrum}
    \State Form source cross-spectral matrix $\mat{C}_q(\omega) \in \mathbb{C}^{M \times M}$:
    \For{each source point $m \in \{1 \dots M\}$}
        \For{each source point $n \in \{1 \dots M\}$}
            \State Compute spatial coherence decay: $\gamma_{mn} \gets \exp\left( -\tilde{d}_{mn} f/f_{\mathrm{ref}} \right)$
            \State Compute incidence-delay phase: $\theta_{mn} \gets \omega b_\alpha (\alpha_m - \alpha_n)$
            \State Evaluate matrix element: $C_{q,mn}(\omega) \gets \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega) \Phi_n(\omega)} \gamma_{mn} \exp(\ii \theta_{mn})$
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \For{each observer direction $\vect{n}$}
        \State Form transfer weight vector $\vect{a} \in \mathbb{C}^M$ where for each source point $m$:
        \State \quad $a_m \gets K_H(\vect{n},\omega) c_m (\vect{l}_m \cdot \vect{n}) w_m \exp(\ii k \vect{n} \cdot \vect{r}_m)$
        \State Evaluate quadratic form: $S_{pp}(\vect{n}, \omega) \gets \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2} \vect{a}^H \mat{C}_q(\omega) \vect{a}$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \Return $\{S_{pp}(\vect{n}, \omega)\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
