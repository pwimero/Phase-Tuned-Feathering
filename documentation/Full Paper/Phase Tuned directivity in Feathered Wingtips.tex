\begin{abstract}
Feathered and serrated lifting-surface edges are often discussed as potential acoustic interference devices, but directivity changes can also arise from simpler mechanisms: source-strength redistribution, local dipole-axis rotation, and altered aerodynamic loading. This paper therefore treats feathered-wingtip directivity control as a mechanism-identification problem rather than as a presumed demonstration of broadband phase steering. The study develops a positive-semidefinite stochastic aeroacoustic array framework for feathered geometries, coupled to arbitrary target-directivity optimization and explicit mechanism ablations. Each feather is represented as a distributed stochastic loading-noise source line, and the far-field pressure power spectral density is evaluated using a Hermitian positive-semidefinite cross-spectral matrix, which guarantees non-negative spectra while separating geometry, source autospectra, coherence, local transfer, and incidence-delay closures. A shared geometry model supports acoustic prediction, visualization, surrogate validation, aerodynamic proxy screening, and CAD-oriented source-grid export. A 50-target campaign with 200 optimization runs is then used to distinguish incidence control, zero-coherence behavior, no-delay behavior, and the full closure model. The key finding is not that arbitrary directivity is synthesized, nor that inter-feather phase interference is proven. Instead, the archived campaign shows repeatable normalized target-shape improvement of approximately $3.3\,\mathrm{dB}$, while the full, no-delay, and zero-coherence ablations are numerically indistinguishable and incidence-only optimization captures nearly all acoustic benefit with a more favourable aerodynamic proxy response. The defensible conclusion is that, under the present closures, feather incidence acts as the dominant directivity-control variable through local projection and source-strength effects; coherent phase mechanisms remain a testable hypothesis requiring closure-sensitivity studies and external validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Feathered wingtips and feathered trailing-edge assemblies are geometrically structured lifting-surface terminations. Their separated elements, sweep variation, incidence variation, taper, and out-of-plane displacement make them acoustically array-like: different source regions can radiate with different amplitudes, orientations, phases, and degrees of mutual coherence. This has made feathered or serrated morphologies attractive as bio-inspired candidates for acoustic control.

However, a phase-interference explanation is not automatic. A change in far-field directivity can be produced by at least three distinct mechanisms: coherent interference between separated sources, incoherent redistribution of source strength, and rotation of local loading dipoles relative to the observer. These mechanisms can produce similar normalized directivity changes in an optimizer. A model that only reports target-fit improvement therefore cannot establish whether phase tuning is actually the operative mechanism.

The purpose of this paper is to formulate feathered-wingtip directivity control as a controlled mechanism-identification problem. The central object is a positive-semidefinite stochastic array model in which the source cross-spectrum is constructed explicitly. This allows coherent, partially coherent, and zero-coherence limits to be evaluated without producing non-physical negative pressure spectra. It also permits geometry, incidence, coherence, and incidence-delay closures to be switched on and off in a reproducible ablation campaign.

The main contribution of the study is therefore narrower, but more defensible, than a broad claim of ``phase-tuned feather noise control.'' The paper contributes a computational framework for asking whether feathered directivity changes are caused by coherent array effects or by lower-order local loading and projection effects. The completed campaign shows that, under the archived closure set, the dominant effect is incidence-controlled directivity shaping rather than demonstrable inter-feather phase steering.

The main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a feathered-wingtip geometry parameterization shared by acoustic prediction, visualization, aerodynamic proxy evaluation, surrogate validation, and CAD-oriented source-grid export;
    \item a positive-semidefinite stochastic cross-spectral formulation for distributed feather-source radiation, ensuring non-negative pressure power spectra for all observer directions and frequencies;
    \item a target-directivity optimization pipeline for arbitrary smooth directivity patterns, reporting target-fit improvement, mutation magnitude, and baseline-relative aerodynamic proxy metrics;
    \item a mechanism-ablation campaign separating incidence-only control, full geometry with zero coherence, full geometry without incidence delay, and the complete closure model;
    \item the campaign-level finding that incidence-only control captures nearly all of the validated normalized-directivity improvement, while the archived full, no-delay, and zero-coherence cases are numerically indistinguishable.
\end{enumerate}

This framing deliberately avoids three overclaims. First, the results do not prove total acoustic-power reduction, because the objective is normalized target-shape matching. Second, they do not constitute experimental or high-fidelity computational validation, because the comparator is a low-cost surrogate. Third, they do not yet prove broadband inter-feather phase steering, because the ablation results do not separate the coherent model from its zero-coherence and no-delay limits. The paper is best read as a reproducible aeroacoustic screening and mechanism-isolation framework, with an incidence-dominated result under the current closures.

\section{Literature}

\subsection{Positioning of the present contribution}

The present work sits between three established research areas: analytical aeroacoustic loading-noise theory, serrated or bio-inspired edge-noise control, and acoustic-array directivity optimization. It does not claim novelty in the existence of trailing-edge noise theory, cross-spectral matrices, or interference effects in serrated edges. Those ideas are well established. Instead, the contribution here is the combination of a feathered-wingtip geometry model, a positive-semidefinite stochastic array construction, arbitrary target-directivity optimization, and a four-way mechanism ablation designed to test whether the optimized directivity changes actually require coherent phase mechanisms.

This distinction is important because prior serrated-edge studies already show that edge geometry can modify directivity and that destructive interference can contribute to noise reduction at particular frequencies and observer angles~\cite{Lyu2016SerratedTE}. A claim that feathered wingtips simply use ``phase interference'' would therefore be weakly distinguished from existing serration theory. The more defensible claim made here is different: when the feathered geometry is optimized under the present closure set, the coherent phase terms do not improve campaign-level target matching relative to the zero-coherence limit, whereas feather incidence alone produces nearly the same validated directivity improvement as the full geometry search. The central result is therefore a mechanism-ablation result for feathered directivity optimization, not an unqualified proof of a new phase-interference phenomenon.

\subsection{Aeroacoustic loading and trailing-edge models}

Classical aerodynamic sound theory provides the physical context for the model. Curle's extension of Lighthill's analogy describes sound generated by unsteady loading on solid boundaries, while acoustic analogy formulations such as FW-H provide routes from unsteady aerodynamic fields to far-field sound. Trailing-edge noise theories and airfoil self-noise models further show how turbulent boundary-layer pressure fluctuations scattered by a trailing edge can radiate broadband sound~\cite{Amiet1976,Brooks1989}. The present work does not attempt to replace these theories. Instead, it uses their structure to motivate a compact transfer kernel and a stochastic source representation for a feathered assembly.

The implemented acoustic model is therefore best interpreted as a closure-based loading-noise surrogate. Its transfer kernel, source autospectrum, coherence law, and incidence-delay law require calibration before absolute sound-pressure-level claims can be made. Without such calibration, the model supports relative comparisons between geometries under a fixed set of assumptions. This is why the reported campaign emphasizes normalized directivity-shape improvement and mechanism separation rather than absolute acoustic certification.

\subsection{Array interpretation of distributed aeroacoustic sources}

Feathered geometries are naturally array-like: they contain multiple source regions separated in space and oriented differently. In coherent or partially coherent conditions, the far-field directivity depends not only on source strength but also on phase differences and cross-spectral relationships between source regions. This motivates a cross-spectral representation in which pressure power spectral density is computed as a quadratic form. Such a form is essential because pressure spectra must remain non-negative for all observer directions and frequencies.

The positive-semidefinite source cross-spectrum is a central methodological constraint and one of the main transferable elements of the paper. A cosine-summed cross-term model can produce negative spectral densities if the source coherence closure is inconsistent. A Hermitian positive-semidefinite matrix construction avoids this failure mode and makes the zero-coherence, no-delay, and full-coherence limits directly comparable. In the present study, that comparability is not merely a numerical convenience; it is what enables the main mechanistic conclusion that the archived directivity improvements do not require off-diagonal coherence terms.

\subsection{Low-order aerodynamic screening}

Fast aerodynamic tools and thin-airfoil-style models are useful for screening incidence ranges, Reynolds-number scales, and approximate lift/drag trends. They are not aeroacoustic validation tools. In this project, low-order aerodynamic quantities are used only as relative performance proxies: lift retention, drag ratio, lift-to-drag retention, separation burden, and spanwise lift-distribution variation. These quantities indicate the aerodynamic cost of directivity-focused geometry mutation, but they should not be presented as experimentally validated aerodynamic coefficients.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Physical assumptions}

The model assumes linear, subsonic, far-field acoustics in a uniform medium with density $\rho_0$, sound speed $c_0$, free-stream speed $U_\infty$, and Mach number
\begin{equation}
    M_\infty = \frac{U_\infty}{c_0}.
\end{equation}
The observer direction is
\begin{equation}
    \vect{n} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    n_x & n_y & n_z
    \end{bmatrix}^{T},
    \qquad
    \|\vect{n}\| = 1.
\end{equation}

Each feather is treated as a rigid prescribed geometry. Porosity, compliance, flutter, structural vibration, and detailed separated-flow dynamics are not resolved. The primary source model is a distributed stochastic loading-noise source line. This makes the framework suitable for testing phase, spacing, incidence, and curvature effects before adding more detailed feather physics.

\subsection{Geometry parameterization}

The research coordinate system uses $x$ as streamwise direction, $y$ as spanwise direction, and $z$ as vertical or wing-normal direction. The CAD coordinate convention is mapped internally so that the same parameter set can generate source grids and CAD-compatible geometry metadata.

For feather $i$, the nondimensional source coordinate is $\eta\in[0,1]$. The acoustic source point is
\begin{equation}
    \vect{r}_i(\eta;\vect{\mu}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    x_i(\eta)\\
    y_i(\eta)\\
    z_i(\eta)
    \end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $\vect{\mu}$ is the design vector. The physical line element is
\begin{equation}
    \dd s_i = J_i(\eta)\dd\eta,
    \qquad
    J_i(\eta)=
    \left\|
        \frac{\partial\vect{r}_i}{\partial\eta}
    \right\|.
\end{equation}
The implementation estimates $J_i$ by finite differences and applies trapezoidal quadrature along each feather.

Each feather has a local chord $c_i(\eta)$, incidence angle, root position, sweep offset, vertical curvature offset, and taper limit. Across feather index, root vertical translation, tip sweep, and tip vertical curvature use endpoint and midpoint controls. For a normalized feather-family coordinate $s\in[0,1]$, a generic cross-feather quantity $g$ is represented by a quadratic profile
\begin{equation}
    g(s)=g_1L_1(s)+g_mL_m(s)+g_7L_7(s),
\end{equation}
where $g_1$, $g_m$, and $g_7$ are first-feather, midpoint, and seventh-feather controls. If the midpoint control is unset, it defaults to the mean of the endpoint values, recovering the original linear trend.

The seven feather incidence values are optimized through an orthonormal cosine-basis representation:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_i =
    \sum_{m=0}^{N-1}
    B_{im}a_m,
\end{equation}
where $a_m$ are incidence shape coefficients and $B_{im}$ is a full-rank basis over feather index. Since the basis is full rank, all seven-feather incidence patterns remain representable, but smooth incidence trends are easier for the optimizer to discover.

\subsection{Three-level acoustic model hierarchy}

The theoretical formulation is built in three levels. Level 1 establishes the compact coherent array limit, Level 2 introduces distributed source lines along each feather, and Level 3 replaces deterministic source amplitudes with a positive-semidefinite stochastic cross-spectral model. This hierarchy is useful because each level exposes a separate physical assumption: compactness, spatial distribution, and stochastic coherence.

\subsubsection{Level 1: compact coherent feather array}

The simplest model treats each feather as one compact acoustic element located at an effective source position $\vect{r}_i$. For observer direction $\vect{n}$ and angular frequency $\omega$, the far-field pressure is
\begin{equation}
    p_1(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    A_i(\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
    \right],
    \qquad
    k=\frac{\omega}{c_0}.
\end{equation}
Here $q_i(\omega)$ is the complex source amplitude of feather $i$, and $A_i$ collects loading direction, local transfer, and any compact-source scaling. This level shows the basic phase-tuning mechanism: changing feather positions or incidence-dependent phase changes the relative phases of the summed terms and therefore changes directivity.

If the compact source amplitudes are deterministic, the pressure power is
\begin{equation}
    |p_1(\vect{n},\omega)|^2
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left|
        \sum_{i=1}^{N}
        A_i q_i
        \exp\left[
            \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
        \right]
    \right|^2.
\end{equation}
This compact model is not the final model, but it provides the array-theory limit that the distributed formulation must recover when each feather is acoustically compact.

\subsubsection{Level 2: deterministic distributed source lines}

Level 2 resolves each feather as a source line parameterized by $\eta\in[0,1]$. The compact source amplitude $q_i$ is replaced by a distributed source amplitude $q_i(\eta,\omega)$, and the effective source position becomes $\vect{r}_i(\eta)$. The far-field pressure is
\begin{equation}
    p_2(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\eta,\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i(\eta)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)\dd\eta.
\end{equation}
The transfer factor $H_i$ includes loading direction and observer-dependent acoustic transfer, while $J_i(\eta)$ converts the nondimensional coordinate to physical arc length. In the compact limit, if $q_i(\eta,\omega)$ and $H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)$ vary slowly over the feather and $\vect{r}_i(\eta)$ can be replaced by an effective position, Level 2 reduces to Level 1.

The deterministic distributed model is useful for checking quadrature convergence and pure phase effects. It is still insufficient for broadband turbulent loading noise because turbulent sources are not phase-locked deterministic amplitudes.

\subsubsection{Level 3: stochastic cross-spectral model}

Level 3 treats the distributed sources as stochastic quantities. The continuous source cross-spectrum is
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        q_i(\eta,\omega)q_j^*(\eta',\omega)
    \right].
\end{equation}
Substituting the distributed pressure expression into the second-order pressure spectrum gives
\begin{align}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    &=
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \sum_{j=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    H_j^*(\eta',\vect{n},\omega)
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    \nonumber\\
    &\quad\times
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot
        \left(
            \vect{r}_i(\eta)-\vect{r}_j(\eta')
        \right)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)J_j(\eta')\dd\eta\dd\eta'.
\end{align}
This is the primary theoretical model used by the paper. It preserves the phase and geometry structure of Level 2 while allowing partial coherence, zero coherence, and full coherence as limiting cases.

\subsubsection{Discretized positive-semidefinite form}

After quadrature, all source points are collected into a vector of length $M$. The pressure spectrum is evaluated as
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega),
\end{equation}
where $\vect{a}$ contains transfer, loading direction, propagation phase, and quadrature weights. The source cross-spectral matrix is
\begin{equation}
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        \vect{q}(\omega)\vect{q}^H(\omega)
    \right].
\end{equation}
If $\mat{C}_q=\mat{C}_q^H$ and $\mat{C}_q\succeq0$, then $S_{pp}\geq0$ for every observer and frequency. This is why the implementation constructs source cross-spectra through a Hermitian positive-semidefinite matrix rather than through unconstrained cosine cross terms.

\subsection{Closure models}

The source cross-spectrum is constructed from autospectra and complex coherence:
\begin{equation}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega)
    =
    \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega).
\end{equation}
The source autospectrum is
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_m(\omega)
    =
    C_q\rho_0^2U_\infty^5c_m^2
    F_\chi(\chi_m)
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
    \chi_m=\frac{\omega c_m S_t}{U_c},
    \qquad
    U_c=\beta U_\infty,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    F_\chi(\chi)=
    \frac{\chi^2}{(1+\chi^2)^{7/3}}.
\end{equation}
The incidence-amplitude closure is
\begin{equation}
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    1+a_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}})^2.
\end{equation}

The coherence magnitude is modeled as
\begin{equation}
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    =
    \exp\left[
        -\frac{\omega}{U_c}
        \left(
            \frac{|\Delta x_{mn}|}{a_x}
            +
            \frac{|\Delta y_{mn}|}{a_y}
            +
            \frac{|\Delta z_{mn}|}{a_z}
        \right)
    \right].
\end{equation}
An incidence-dependent delay is represented by
\begin{equation}
    \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}}),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega)
    =
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    \exp\left[
        \ii\omega
        \left(
            \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)-\tau_\alpha(\alpha_n)
        \right)
    \right].
\end{equation}

The transfer weight for source point $m$ is
\begin{equation}
    a_m(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    c_m
    \left(
        \vect{d}_m\cdot\vect{n}
    \right)
    w_m
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m
    \right],
\end{equation}
with implemented kernel
\begin{equation}
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{\omega/c_0}{(1-M_\infty n_x)^2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Surrogate aeroacoustic validation}

The optimized geometry is evaluated against a separate low-cost surrogate simulator. The theoretical model used by the optimizer and the simulator used for validation are intentionally not identical. The theoretical model uses the positive-semidefinite cross-spectral formulation above, with compact closure laws for source autospectra, coherence, transfer weighting, and incidence delay. The surrogate simulator uses the same geometry and observer definitions, but it replaces the optimizer's source-amplitude closure with BPM/TNO-inspired components: turbulent-boundary-layer trailing-edge contribution, separated-flow contribution, bluntness contribution, and a tip-vortex contribution. Each component uses a log-Gaussian spectral shape in a Strouhal-like variable.

The local aerodynamic state for each source segment uses a thin-airfoil lift proxy with soft stall attenuation:
\begin{equation}
    C_l =
    2\pi\alpha
    \left[
        1+
        \left(
            \frac{|\alpha|}{\alpha_{\mathrm{stall}}}
        \right)^8
    \right]^{-1},
\end{equation}
and a drag proxy
\begin{equation}
    C_d =
    C_{d0}+k_dC_l^2.
\end{equation}
The surrogate is not high-fidelity CFD and is not experimental validation. Its role is to provide an independent, internally consistent comparison layer. Agreement between the theoretical prediction and the surrogate simulation tests whether the geometry-to-directivity mechanism transfers across two different source closures. In this sense, theory--surrogate agreement supports the internal reliability of the formulation, but it does not prove absolute aeroacoustic accuracy against physical data.

\subsection{Target generation and fit metrics}

Each arbitrary target directivity pattern is generated from one to three smooth lobes on the observer sphere:
\begin{equation}
    T_o =
    \sum_{\ell=1}^{N_\ell}
    A_\ell
    \left[
        \frac{1+\vect{n}_o\cdot\vect{c}_\ell}{2}
    \right]^{p_\ell}.
\end{equation}
The target is peak normalized in decibels:
\begin{equation}
    T_o^{\dB}
    =
    10\log_{10}
    \left(
        \frac{T_o}{\max_o T_o}
    \right).
\end{equation}

For a candidate geometry, the predicted directivity is also peak normalized. The optimizer minimizes
\begin{equation}
    J_{\mathrm{fit}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left(
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}
        -
        \hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right)^2.
\end{equation}
This value is used internally by the optimizer, but the paper reports more interpretable target-fit metrics:
\begin{align}
    E_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
    &=
    \left[
        \frac{1}{N_o}
        \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
        \left(
            \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
        \right)^2
    \right]^{1/2},\\
    E_{\mathrm{MAE}}
    &=
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left|
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right|.
\end{align}
Peak-direction error is also reported:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{peak}}
    =
    \cos^{-1}
    \left(
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{target\,peak}}
        \cdot
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{predicted\,peak}}
    \right).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Aerodynamic tradeoff metrics}

The aerodynamic cost of mutation is evaluated relative to the reference feathered geometry. For each source segment $m$, define
\begin{equation}
    A_m^*=c_m\Delta s_m.
\end{equation}
Integrated lift and drag proxies are
\begin{align}
    L^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{l,m}A_m^*,\\
    D^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{d,m}A_m^*.
\end{align}
The lift-to-drag proxy is
\begin{equation}
    \left(\frac{L}{D}\right)^*
    =
    \frac{L^*}{D^*}.
\end{equation}
The reported baseline-relative quantities are
\begin{align}
    R_L &= \frac{L^*_{\mathrm{opt}}}{L^*_{\mathrm{base}}},\\
    R_D &= \frac{D^*_{\mathrm{opt}}}{D^*_{\mathrm{base}}},\\
    R_{L/D} &=
    \frac{(L/D)^*_{\mathrm{opt}}}{(L/D)^*_{\mathrm{base}}}.
\end{align}
These are treated as signed quantities because large incidence mutations can reverse the integrated lift proxy.

\subsection{Mechanism ablation campaign}

To isolate the contributions of specific physical mechanisms to far-field directivity shaping, we formulate a mechanism ablation study comprising four distinct optimization configurations. Each target directivity pattern in the campaign is optimized from scratch under all four configurations, allowing a direct comparison of their performance:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Incidence only} (\texttt{incidence}): Only the 7 incidence shape coefficients are optimized, while the spatial position, spacing, sweep, curvature, and taper of the feathers are fully locked to their baseline values. This isolates the steering contribution of pure chordwise camber-tuning.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, zero coherence} (\texttt{zero\_coherence}): The full 18-variable design vector is optimized, but the acoustic cross-spectral density matrix is forced to be diagonal (i.e., $\Gamma_{mn} = \delta_{mn}$), representing completely uncorrelated source regions. This isolates the role of spatial shielding and loading projection changes from coherent phase-tuning.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, no delay} (\texttt{no\_delay}): The full 18-variable design vector is optimized, but the local incidence-dependent acoustic delay is forced to zero (i.e., $\tau_\alpha = 0$), keeping the spatial and coherence elements active but neutralizing local aerodynamic phase delays.
    \item \textbf{Full model} (\texttt{full}): The full 18-variable design vector is optimized under the complete theoretical acoustic model, combining spatial positioning, out-of-plane curvature, coherent array tuning, and local incidence-dependent delays.
\end{enumerate}
This independent, multi-level formulation allows the campaign analysis to systematically distinguish between geometric steering, coherent interference, and local aerodynamic delay tuning.

\subsection{Optimization and numerical implementation}

The genetic optimizer searches an 18-variable design vector. Seven variables are incidence shape coefficients. The remaining variables control feather spacing, endpoint and midpoint root vertical translations, endpoint and midpoint tip sweep, endpoint and midpoint tip vertical curvature, and minimum tip-chord scale.

% We removed the warm-starting chain between levels to make the ablation studies truly independent.
Optimization uses a differential-evolution-style population search. The population size is
\begin{equation}
    P=N_pN_\mu,
\end{equation}
where $N_p$ is the population multiplier and $N_\mu$ is the number of design variables. Candidate mutation uses three distinct parent vectors and a random mutation factor. Trial vectors replace existing vectors only when they improve the objective. The default stopping rule is patience based: optimization stops after a specified number of generations without improvement.

The only direct geometry penalty is projected source-line intersection. Candidate source lines are checked on a coarse source grid; intersections receive a large penalty. This keeps the search broad while preventing self-crossing geometries that cannot be interpreted cleanly.

Each generation is evaluated in two stages. All candidates are first scored on a coarse acoustic grid with reduced source quadrature, strided observers, and strided frequencies. The best fraction is then re-evaluated at the full genetic-algorithm resolution. Final surrogate validation is separate from the genetic search and uses a denser observer grid and broader frequency set:
\begin{equation}
    f\in\{250,500,1000,2000,4000,8000\}\ \mathrm{Hz}.
\end{equation}

The implementation is organized as a Python research package. The core modules are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{geometry}, \texttt{closures}, \texttt{acoustics}, and \texttt{acoustics\_torch};
    \item \texttt{simulation}, \texttt{ga\_optimization}, and \texttt{pipeline\_ga};
    \item \texttt{aggregate\_campaign} and \texttt{visualization}.
\end{itemize}
The PyTorch acoustic path is heavily optimized for hardware-accelerated batch execution on modern graphics processing units, including Apple Metal Performance Shaders (MPS). Rather than explicitly constructing and allocating the full four-dimensional cross-spectral density matrix $\mat{C}_q(\omega)$ of size $(P, F, N, N)$—which incurs severe memory bandwidth and allocation penalties—the implementation pre-scales the observer-side transfer weights $\vect{a}_m$ directly by the source autospectrum square root $\sqrt{\Phi_m}$. This mathematically exact refactoring reduces the memory footprint from quadratic in the source grid size ($O(N^2)$) to linear ($O(NO)$), where the number of observer directions $O$ is typically much smaller than $N$.

Furthermore, high-overhead tensor contraction operations (\texttt{torch.einsum}) are replaced with native batched matrix-matrix multiplications ($\vect{K}_H @ \vect{n}^T$ and $\vect{r} @ \vect{n}^T$), which map directly to Apple Silicon's hardware matrix acceleration units (AMX/MPS). For the \texttt{zero\_coherence} ablation level, the diagonal cross-spectral matrix formulation enables a specialized fast path that completely bypasses spatial distance calculations and matrix multiplications, evaluating far-field pressure power as a simple element-wise square-and-sum of scaled transfer weights:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) = \sum_{m} \left( |a_{m,\mathrm{real}}|^2 + |a_{m,\mathrm{imag}}|^2 \right),
\end{equation}
which accelerates optimization steps by orders of magnitude. Geometry generation and intersection checks remain CPU-side, while batched acoustic evaluation is performed on the device.

\section{Results}

This section reports the completed generalized-directivity campaign. The campaign used 50 randomly generated target directivity patterns, with seeds 1--50 and one-, two-, or three-lobe target classes. Each target was optimized under four mechanism settings: \emph{Incidence-only} ($\mathtt{incidence}$), \emph{Full geometry with zero coherence} ($\mathtt{zero\_coherence}$), \emph{Full geometry with no incidence-delay closure} ($\mathtt{no\_delay}$), and the \emph{Full model} ($\mathtt{full}$). Thus, the campaign contains 200 optimization runs.

The main result is a mechanism result, not a broad phase-steering result. The optimized geometries repeatedly reduce normalized target-shape error, but the improvement is almost entirely recoverable without full geometry mutation and without off-diagonal source coherence. The nominal baseline geometry had a mean target-shape RMSE of $10.90\pm2.77\,\mathrm{dB}$ across the 50 targets. After optimization, the mean validated surrogate RMSE was approximately $7.53$--$7.56\,\mathrm{dB}$ depending on the mechanism level, corresponding to an average improvement of approximately $3.33$--$3.37\,\mathrm{dB}$. The fact that these improvements persist in the zero-coherence and incidence-only tests is the basis for the paper's incidence-dominated interpretation.

The difference between incidence-only and full-geometry optimization is small: the full-geometry cases improve the mean validated target RMSE by only $0.035\,\mathrm{dB}$ relative to incidence-only. This difference is much smaller than the run-to-run standard deviation of the improvement metric, which is approximately $1.1\,\mathrm{dB}$. The defensible interpretation is therefore that, for this target set and closure configuration, additional planform and out-of-plane geometric degrees of freedom did not produce a practically meaningful acoustic advantage over incidence control alone.

\subsection{Mechanism ablation: coherence and incidence delay}

The most important claim in the results comes from the mechanism ablation rather than from the raw target-fit reduction. The $\mathtt{zero\_coherence}$, $\mathtt{no\_delay}$, and $\mathtt{full}$ cases give identical campaign statistics in Table~\ref{tab:campaign_results}. Inspection of the saved campaign data shows equality to numerical precision for the validated target RMSE, target-improvement metric, drag ratio, lift-to-drag proxy, mutation magnitude, and incidence RMS change for every seed.

This equality changes the interpretation of the work. It does \emph{not} support a strong claim that the optimized directivity patterns are produced by broadband inter-feather phase interference. In the archived implementation, the default incidence-delay coefficient is zero unless explicitly changed, so the $\mathtt{full}$ and $\mathtt{no\_delay}$ conditions are expected to coincide under the stored closure configuration. In addition, the exponential coherence closure decays as
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_{mn}(f)=
    \exp\left[-\frac{\omega}{U_c}
    \left(
    \frac{|\Delta x_{mn}|}{L_x}+
    \frac{|\Delta y_{mn}|}{L_y}+
    \frac{|\Delta z_{mn}|}{L_z}
    \right)\right],
\end{equation}
where $U_c=\beta U_\infty$ and the default coherence lengths are $L_x=L_y=L_z=0.30\,\mathrm{m}$. With $U_\infty=30\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ and $\beta=0.7$, the convective velocity is $U_c=21\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. At the validation frequencies used here, modest inter-source separations can therefore suppress off-diagonal coherence strongly. Consequently, the zero-coherence and exponential-coherence cases can become numerically indistinguishable for this geometry and frequency range.

The defensible interpretation is narrow but valuable: under the archived closure parameters, target-shape improvement is dominated by diagonal source-strength and local transfer effects, especially incidence-dependent loading direction and chord/source weighting. This is a negative result for the stronger phase-steering hypothesis, but it is a positive result for the framework. The ablation demonstrates that the model can distinguish a mechanism that merely sounds plausible from one that is actually active under the chosen assumptions. A stronger phase-tuning claim would require sensitivity studies in which coherence length, convective velocity, and nonzero incidence-delay coefficients are varied deliberately, and in which the full model separates measurably from the zero-coherence and no-delay ablations.

\subsection{Aerodynamic proxy tradeoff}

The aerodynamic proxies favour incidence-only optimization. Incidence-only optimization gives a mean drag ratio of $0.639$ and a mean lift-to-drag retention of $+5.96\%$. The full-geometry cases give a mean drag ratio of $0.694$ and a mean lift-to-drag retention of $-2.81\%$. Thus, while both mechanism classes reduce the drag proxy relative to the baseline, incidence-only optimization has the better combined acoustic--aerodynamic behaviour in this campaign.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Main implication of the study}

The main contribution of the paper is the combination of formulation and diagnosis. The positive-semidefinite stochastic array model makes feathered geometries analyzable without permitting non-physical negative spectra. The target-directivity optimizer then provides a way to test controllability across many target patterns rather than one selected case. Most importantly, the ablation campaign tests whether the directivity improvement depends on coherent phase mechanisms. Under the archived closures, it does not. This makes the study's main result a defensible mechanism-isolation result: incidence-tuned local loading and transfer effects dominate the optimized normalized directivity response.

This reframing is stronger than a generic bio-inspired noise-control claim. It makes the work falsifiable. If future calibrated closures produce a measurable separation between the full and zero-coherence models, the same framework can quantify the onset of coherent phase control. If they do not, the framework still identifies incidence as the simpler and more robust control variable.

\subsection{What the campaign demonstrates}

The campaign demonstrates that the implemented model can adapt normalized directivity shapes by changing feather parameters. The average validated improvement of approximately $3.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ is large enough to be meaningful within the model, and the result is repeated across 50 independent target seeds. The optimization is therefore not merely fitting a single favourable example.

The most robust physical interpretation is amplitude-and-transfer shaping rather than coherent phase-array steering. Incidence changes alter loading direction, source autospectrum weighting, and observer projection. These effects remain active even when cross-source coherence is zero. Because the zero-coherence, no-delay, and full-geometry results coincide under the archived settings, the results should not be described as evidence that inter-feather phase delays are the dominant control mechanism.

\subsection{What remains uncertain}

Several claims should be avoided or qualified. First, the results do not show that feathered wingtips reduce total acoustic power; the objective is normalized target-shape matching. A geometry could improve directivity fit while leaving total radiated power unchanged or even increasing it. Second, the present surrogate does not resolve boundary-layer turbulence, feather porosity, compliance, or vortex--structure interaction. Third, the aerodynamic tradeoff is based on low-order proxies. The drag ratio and lift-to-drag retention are useful for ranking candidate geometries inside this framework, but they should not be interpreted as experimentally validated aerodynamic performance.

The exact equality among the three full-geometry ablation levels is also a diagnostic result. It indicates either that the off-diagonal coherence and incidence-delay mechanisms are physically negligible under the chosen closure scales, or that the closure configuration suppresses the very mechanism the title emphasizes. The current evidence favours the latter as a serious possibility because the incidence-delay coefficient is zero in the default closure set. A revised phase-tuning study should therefore include a designed closure-sensitivity matrix before making broad claims about phase-tuned directivity.

\subsection{Mathematical mechanism of incidence steering}

The observation that incidence optimization captures nearly all of the acoustic target-fit improvement can be mathematically justified by examining the zero-coherence limit of the positive-semidefinite array model. When the spanwise separation exceeds the turbulent correlation length, the off-diagonal coherence terms vanish ($\Gamma_{mn} = 0$ for $m \neq n$). The double summation in the stochastic cross-spectral matrix formulation strictly collapses to an incoherent sum over the diagonal source-spectrum terms:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) \approx \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \int_0^1 \left| H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega) \right|^2 \Phi_{qq}^{ii}(\eta,\omega) J_i(\eta) \dd \eta.
\end{equation}
In this limit, inter-feather phase delay $\exp(\ii k\vect{n}\cdot\Delta\vect{r})$ exerts mathematically zero influence on the far-field pressure. The optimizer must therefore shape the far-field directivity by manipulating the individual feather terms.

The incidence angle $\alpha_i$ acts as a dual-control mechanism on these diagonal terms. First, it dictates the \emph{kinematic projection} of the acoustic dipole. The transfer function $H_i$ projects the local unsteady lift vector $\vect{l}_i(\eta)$ onto the observer direction $\vect{n}$:
\begin{equation}
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega) \propto \vect{n} \cdot \vect{l}_i(\eta).
\end{equation}
Rotating the feather by $\alpha_i$ physically rotates $\vect{l}_i(\eta)$, steering the geometric nulls and peaks of the individual dipole lobes toward or away from the target sectors.

Second, the incidence angle acts as an \emph{aerodynamic scaling} mechanism. Changing $\alpha_i$ modifies the local steady loading, the boundary layer thickness $\delta^*(\eta)$, and the effective convective velocity $U_c$, all of which fundamentally dictate the turbulent autospectrum $\Phi_{qq}^{ii}(\eta,\omega)$. 

Consequently, the genetic algorithm does not need to execute complex spatial morphing to create phased-array interference. Instead, it solves the directivity problem by locally rotating dipole lobes (via $H_i$) and independently throttling their source intensities (via $\Phi_{qq}^{ii}$). This dual-control nature of incidence perfectly explains why the $\mathtt{incidence}$-only ablation achieves the same acoustic performance as full geometric morphing when physical coherence is naturally constrained.

\subsection{Recommended additional analyses}

Three follow-up analyses would make the phase-mechanism question substantially stronger. First, sweep the coherence lengths, convective velocity, and incidence-delay coefficient over physically plausible ranges and report the parameter regions in which the full model separates from the zero-coherence and no-delay ablations. This would convert the present negative ablation result into a phase-sensitivity map. Second, include an energy-normalized metric, such as change in integrated radiated power or sector suppression at fixed total power, so that directivity redistribution is not conflated with source-level scaling. Third, repeat the target campaign with stratified target classes whose baseline RMSE distributions are matched across lobe counts. This would separate controllability from initial target difficulty.

A fourth useful extension would be external validation against either resolved computational aeroacoustics or controlled wind-tunnel measurements. The present surrogate comparison is valuable for checking internal consistency, but it cannot establish physical accuracy of the closure parameters. External data would be needed to determine whether real feathered or feather-inspired geometries operate in the incidence-dominated regime found here or in a coherence-sensitive regime where phase effects become measurable.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper recasts feathered-wingtip directivity control as a positive-semidefinite stochastic aeroacoustic array problem and uses that formulation to test mechanism, not merely target fit. The framework combines a shared feathered geometry model, a distributed loading-noise source representation, a Hermitian positive-semidefinite cross-spectral matrix, arbitrary smooth target-directivity optimization, surrogate validation, and aerodynamic proxy screening.

The completed 50-target campaign shows repeatable normalized target-directivity improvement of approximately $3.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ under the archived model assumptions. The clearest finding is that this improvement is incidence-dominated. Incidence-only optimization achieves almost the same validated target-fit improvement as the full 18-variable geometry search, while giving better aerodynamic proxy behaviour: a mean drag ratio of $0.639$ and mean lift-to-drag retention of $+5.96\%$, compared with $0.694$ and $-2.81\%$ for the full-geometry cases.

The paper therefore does not claim experimental proof of broadband inter-feather phase steering. In the archived campaign, the full, no-delay, and zero-coherence ablations are identical, and the default incidence-delay coefficient is zero. The correct conclusion is conditional: within this positive-semidefinite surrogate framework and closure set, feather incidence can tune normalized directivity effectively through local projection and source-strength effects, while coherent phase mechanisms remain unconfirmed. The broader contribution is a reproducible framework for separating those mechanisms in future calibrated studies.

\section{Evaluation of the PSD Matrix}

The following pseudocode outlines the implementation used to evaluate the far-field pressure power spectral density across a set of observer directions and frequencies. It illustrates how the double-summation structure and local spanwise numerical integration are aggregated.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Evaluation of Positive-Semidefinite Array Far-Field Pressure Spectrum}
\label{alg:psd_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Observer set $\{\mathbf{n}\}$, Frequency set $\{\omega\}$, Array geometry $\{\mathbf{r}_i(\eta), \mathbf{l}_i(\eta), J_i(\eta), \alpha_i\}$, Integration nodes $\{\eta_q\}$ and weights $\{w_q\}$
\Ensure Far-field acoustic pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}(\mathbf{n}, \omega)$
\For{each frequency $\omega$}
    \For{each observer $\mathbf{n}$}
        \State Initialize total PSD: $S_{pp} \gets 0$
        \For{each feather $m \in \{1 \dots N\}$}
            \For{each feather $n \in \{1 \dots N\}$}
                \State Compute coherence decay $\Gamma_{mn}$ based on spatial separation $|\mathbf{r}_m - \mathbf{r}_n|$
                \State Compute incidence-dependent aerodynamic phase delay $\tau_{\alpha}$
                \State Form complex cross-term correlation: $C_{mn} \gets \Gamma_{mn} \exp(-\ii \omega \tau_{\alpha})$
                \State Initialize spanwise integral term: $I_{mn} \gets 0$
                \For{each integration node $\eta_q$}
                    \State Compute transfer $H_m \propto \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}_m(\eta_q) \exp(\ii k \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r}_m(\eta_q))$
                    \State Compute transfer $H_n^* \propto \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}_n(\eta_q) \exp(-\ii k \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r}_n(\eta_q))$
                    \State Retrieve local source autospectra $\Phi_{qq}^{mm}(\eta_q)$ and $\Phi_{qq}^{nn}(\eta_q)$
                    \State Calculate cross-source amplitude $A \gets \sqrt{\Phi_{qq}^{mm} \Phi_{qq}^{nn}}$
                    \State Accumulate: $I_{mn} \gets I_{mn} + H_m H_n^* A J_m(\eta_q) J_n(\eta_q) w_q$
                \EndFor
                \State Add to total observer spectrum: $S_{pp} \gets S_{pp} + C_{mn} I_{mn}$
            \EndFor
        \EndFor
        \State Normalize geometrically: $S_{pp} \gets S_{pp} / (4\pi R)^2$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \Return $S_{pp}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
