\begin{abstract}
Feathered wingtip directivity changes are often attributed to broadband phase steering, but they can also arise from source-strength redistribution, local dipole rotation, and altered aerodynamic loading. This study treats feathered-wingtip directivity control as a mechanism-identification problem. We develop a positive-semidefinite stochastic aeroacoustic array framework using a Hermitian cross-spectral matrix to guarantee non-negative pressure spectra while isolating geometry, source strength, coherence, local transfer, and incidence-delay effects. In a 50-target optimization campaign, three full-geometry configurations all outperformed incidence-only control by approximately $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ in validated surrogate RMSE. However, the complete model, a no-delay model, and a zero-coherence model were statistically indistinguishable, showing that geometry-sensitive directivity changes are driven primarily by source-strength redistribution and dipole rotation rather than phase interference. Comparison with a separate boundary-layer self-noise surrogate yields a calibration RMSE of $2.42\,\mathrm{dB}$ and a validation RMSE of $5.70\,\mathrm{dB}$, with errors increasing significantly above $kc_{\mathrm{ref}} \approx 11$. The framework serves as a low-to-mid-frequency screening tool for comparing geometry-sensitive directivity mechanisms rather than an absolute high-frequency sound predictor.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Structured lifting-surface terminations like feathered wingtips are often modeled as acoustic arrays where spatial spacing, sweep, out-of-plane displacement, and incidence vary across elements. While these features suggest bio-inspired noise control through phase interference, directivity changes can also arise from incoherent source-strength redistribution or dipole axis rotation. Models reporting only overall target-fit improvements cannot distinguish between these mechanisms.

This paper addresses this ambiguity by formulating feathered-wingtip directivity control as a mechanism-identification problem. We establish a positive-semidefinite stochastic array model that allows coherent, partially coherent, and zero-coherence limits to be compared without generating non-physical negative pressure spectra. This enables systematic ablation of geometry, coherence, and phase-delay closures.

The primary contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A unified geometry parameterization shared across prediction, validation, aerodynamic proxy screening, and CAD export.
    \item A positive-semidefinite stochastic cross-spectral formulation ensuring non-negative pressure power spectra.
    \item A target-directivity optimization pipeline for arbitrary smooth radiation patterns.
    \item A mechanism-ablation study comparing incidence-only control, full geometry with zero coherence, full geometry without incidence delay, and the complete closure model.
    \item A 50-target optimization campaign showing that full-geometry mutations outperform incidence-only control by $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ in validated RMSE, but showing no statistical difference among the three full-geometry ablation levels.
\end{enumerate}

We emphasize that this framework evaluates normalized target-shape matching rather than absolute acoustic power reduction, uses a low-fidelity validation surrogate, and demonstrates geometry-sensitive control rather than proving broadband inter-feather phase steering.

\section{Literature Review}

This work builds upon three established research areas: analytical aeroacoustic loading-noise theory, bio-inspired edge-noise control, and acoustic-array optimization. Classical aerodynamic sound theories, including Curle's loading noise extension of Lighthill's analogy~\cite{Curle1955} and Farassat's formulations~\cite{Farassat2007}, describe acoustic radiation from unsteady surface forces. Trailing-edge and self-noise models~\cite{Amiet1976,Howe1978,Brooks1989} establish how turbulent boundary-layer scattering radiates broadband noise. Furthermore, prior studies on serrated edges demonstrate that edge geometry alters directivity and that destructive interference can reduce far-field noise at specific frequencies and angles~\cite{Lyu2016SerratedTE}.

Rather than proposing a new acoustic analogy, we implement a closure-based loading-noise surrogate to evaluate relative directivity changes. Because feathered geometries consist of spatially separated, differently oriented source regions, the far-field radiation depends on both local source strength and spatial cross-coherence. While ad-hoc cosine-summed cross-term models can yield non-physical negative power spectral densities (PSDs) under inconsistent coherence closures, we employ a Hermitian positive-semidefinite matrix construction. This formulation guarantees non-negative spectra across all frequencies and observer directions, enabling mathematically consistent comparisons between the zero-coherence, no-delay, and full-coherence limits.

Finally, we utilize thin-airfoil lift and drag proxies as relative screening metrics. These proxies estimate the aerodynamic penalties associated with directivity-optimized mutations without asserting absolute aerodynamic performance.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Physical assumptions and geometry representation}

We assume linear, subsonic, far-field acoustics in a uniform medium with density $\rho_0$, sound speed $c_0$, free-stream speed $U_\infty$, and Mach number
\begin{equation}
    M_\infty = \frac{U_\infty}{c_0}.
\label{eq:mach}
\end{equation}
The observer direction is defined by the unit vector:
\begin{equation}
    \vect{n} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    n_x & n_y & n_z
    \end{bmatrix}^{T},
    \qquad
    \|\vect{n}\| = 1.
\end{equation}
Feathers are modeled as rigid geometries represented by distributed stochastic loading-noise source lines. Structural vibrations, compliance, and porosity are neglected.

For feather $i$, the coordinate $\eta \in [0, 1]$ parameterizes the source point:
\begin{equation}
    \vect{r}_i(\eta;\vect{\mu}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    x_i(\eta)\\
    y_i(\eta)\\
    z_i(\eta)
    \end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:source_position}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{\mu}$ is the design vector. The arc length element is $\dd s_i = J_i(\eta)\dd\eta$, with Jacobian:
\begin{equation}
    J_i(\eta)=
    \left\|
        \frac{\partial\vect{r}_i}{\partial\eta}
    \right\|.
\end{equation}
We estimate $J_i$ via finite differences and apply trapezoidal quadrature. 

Root vertical translation, tip sweep, and tip vertical curvature across the feathers $s \in [0, 1]$ are parameterized by quadratic profiles:
\begin{equation}
    g(s)=g_1L_1(s)+g_mL_m(s)+g_7L_7(s),
\end{equation}
where $g_1$, $g_m$, and $g_7$ represent control points for the first, middle, and seventh feathers. The seven feather incidence values $\alpha_i$ are represented in an orthonormal cosine basis:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_i =
    \sum_{m=0}^{N-1}
    B_{im}a_m,
\label{eq:incidence_basis}
\end{equation}
where $a_m$ are incidence coefficients and $B_{im}$ is a full-rank basis.

\subsection{Acoustic model hierarchy}

We formulate the aeroacoustic model in three levels to isolate physical assumptions of compactness, spatial distribution, and coherence.

\subsubsection{Level 1: compact coherent feather array}
Each feather is treated as a compact source at $\vect{r}_i$. For observer direction $\vect{n}$ and frequency $\omega$, the far-field pressure and deterministic power are:
\begin{equation}
    p_1(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    A_i(\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
    \right],
    \qquad
    k=\frac{\omega}{c_0},
\label{eq:compact_array_pressure}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    |p_1(\vect{n},\omega)|^2
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left|
        \sum_{i=1}^{N}
        A_i q_i
        \exp\left[
            \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i
        \right]
    \right|^2,
\end{equation}
where $q_i(\omega)$ is the complex source amplitude, and $A_i$ accounts for local loading and transfer.

\subsubsection{Level 2: deterministic distributed source lines}
To account for spatial distribution, we integrate along each feather's source line:
\begin{equation}
    p_2(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{4\pi R}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    q_i(\eta,\omega)
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_i(\eta)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)\dd\eta,
\label{eq:distributed_pressure}
\end{equation}
where $H_i$ collects loading direction and propagation transfer. 

\subsubsection{Level 3: stochastic cross-spectral model}
To represent turbulent loading noise, we define the source cross-spectrum:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        q_i(\eta,\omega)q_j^*(\eta',\omega)
    \right].
\label{eq:continuous_cross_spectrum}
\end{equation}
The far-field pressure power spectral density (PSD) becomes:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:continuous_psd}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    &=
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \sum_{j=1}^{N}
    \int_0^1
    \int_0^1
    H_i(\eta,\vect{n},\omega)
    H_j^*(\eta',\vect{n},\omega)
    \Phi_{qq}^{ij}(\eta,\eta',\omega)
    \nonumber\\
    &\quad\times
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot
        \left(
            \vect{r}_i(\eta)-\vect{r}_j(\eta')
        \right)
    \right]
    J_i(\eta)J_j(\eta')\dd\eta\dd\eta'.
\end{align}

Discretizing the quadrature yields the quadratic form:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \vect{a}^{H}(\vect{n},\omega)
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    \vect{a}(\vect{n},\omega),
\label{eq:discrete_psd}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{a}$ aggregates transfer factors, loading orientation, phase, and weights. The source cross-spectral matrix is:
\begin{equation}
    \mat{C}_q(\omega)
    =
    \E\left[
        \vect{q}(\omega)\vect{q}^H(\omega)
    \right].
\end{equation}
By constructing $\mat{C}_q$ as Hermitian positive-semidefinite ($\mat{C}_q \succeq 0$), we mathematically guarantee non-negative pressure spectra ($S_{pp} \geq 0$) for all observer angles and frequencies.

\subsection{Closure models and nondimensional regime}

The source cross-spectral matrix is closed using:
\begin{equation}
    C_{q,mn}(\omega)
    =
    \sqrt{\Phi_m(\omega)\Phi_n(\omega)}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega),
\label{eq:source_cross_spectrum}
\end{equation}
where the source autospectrum is:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_m(\omega)
    =
    C_q\rho_0^2U_\infty^5c_m^2
    F_\chi(\chi_m)
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m),
\label{eq:source_autospectrum}
\end{equation}
with:
\begin{equation}
    \chi_m=\frac{\omega c_m S_t}{U_c},
    \qquad
    U_c=\beta U_\infty,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    F_\chi(\chi)=
    \frac{\chi^2}{(1+\chi^2)^{7/3}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    F_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    1+a_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}})^2.
\end{equation}

The spatial coherence magnitude and incidence-dependent phase delay are modeled as:
\begin{equation}
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    =
    \exp\left[
        -\frac{f}{f_{\mathrm{ref}}}
        \sqrt{
            \left(\frac{\Delta x_{mn}}{a_x}\right)^2
            +
            \left(\frac{\Delta y_{mn}}{a_y}\right)^2
            +
            \left(\frac{\Delta z_{mn}}{a_z}\right)^2
        }
    \right],
\label{eq:coherence_magnitude}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)
    =
    b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}}),
\label{eq:incidence_delay}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_{mn}(\omega)
    =
    |\Gamma_{mn}(\omega)|
    \exp\left[
        \ii\omega
        \left(
            \tau_\alpha(\alpha_m)-\tau_\alpha(\alpha_n)
        \right)
    \right].
\label{eq:complex_coherence}
\end{equation}
The baseline closures ($a_x=a_y=0.9\,\mathrm{m}$, $a_z=0.3\,\mathrm{m}$, $f_{\mathrm{ref}}=1500\,\mathrm{Hz}$, $\beta=0.85$, and $b_\alpha=5.0\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{s\,rad^{-1}}$) ensure neighboring feathers remain partially coherent at low-to-mid frequencies while maintaining active phase shifts.

We analyze the system using the center-wing chord $c_{\mathrm{ref}} = 0.30\,\mathrm{m}$ to define nondimensional groups:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\vect{r}}=\frac{\vect{r}}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}},
    \qquad
    \kappa = kc_{\mathrm{ref}} = \frac{2\pi f c_{\mathrm{ref}}}{c_0},
    \qquad
    \tilde{a}_j = \frac{a_j}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}},
    \qquad
    \Pi_\alpha = \frac{U_\infty b_\alpha}{c_{\mathrm{ref}}}.
\label{eq:nondim_groups}
\end{equation}
The coherence decay is governed by the dimensionless separation:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{d}_{mn}
    =
    \sqrt{
        \left(\frac{\Delta x_{mn}}{a_x}\right)^2
        +
        \left(\frac{\Delta y_{mn}}{a_y}\right)^2
        +
        \left(\frac{\Delta z_{mn}}{a_z}\right)^2
    },
\label{eq:nondim_coherence_distance}
\end{equation}
and the delay phase group:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta_{mn}
    =
    \omega b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_n).
\label{eq:delay_phase_group}
\end{equation}

The transfer weight for source point $m$ is:
\begin{equation}
    a_m(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    c_m
    \left(
        \vect{d}_m\cdot\vect{n}
    \right)
    w_m
    \exp\left[
        \ii k\vect{n}\cdot\vect{r}_m
    \right],
\label{eq:transfer_weight}
\end{equation}
with kernel:
\begin{equation}
    K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{\omega/c_0}{(1-M_\infty n_x)^2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Surrogate validation, target fit, and aerodynamic proxies}

We validate optimized geometries using a separate boundary-layer self-noise surrogate simulator. This simulator uses BPM/TNO-inspired spectral closures (trailing-edge, separation, bluntness, and tip-vortex contributions) rather than the optimizer's compact loading-noise closures. The local aerodynamic state relies on a thin-airfoil lift proxy with soft stall:
\begin{equation}
    C_l =
    2\pi\alpha
    \left[
        1+
        \left(
            \frac{|\alpha|}{\alpha_{\mathrm{stall}}}
        \right)^8
    \right]^{-1},
\label{eq:lift_proxy}
\end{equation}
and drag coefficient $C_d = C_{d0}+k_dC_l^2$.

Target directivity patterns $T_o$ are generated with $N_\ell = 1, 2,$ or $3$ smooth lobes:
\begin{equation}
    T_o =
    \sum_{\ell=1}^{N_\ell}
    A_\ell
    \left[
        \frac{1+\vect{n}_o\cdot\vect{c}_\ell}{2}
    \right]^{p_\ell}.
\label{eq:target_generation}
\end{equation}
The optimizer minimizes the mean-squared error of peak-normalized directivities:
\begin{equation}
    J_{\mathrm{fit}}
    =
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left(
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}
        -
        \hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right)^2.
\label{eq:fit_objective}
\end{equation}
We report target-fit and direction errors:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:fit_metrics}
    E_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
    &=
    \left[
        \frac{1}{N_o}
        \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
        \left(
            \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
        \right)^2
    \right]^{1/2},\\
    E_{\mathrm{MAE}}
    &=
    \frac{1}{N_o}
    \sum_{o=1}^{N_o}
    \left|
        \hat{S}_o^{\dB}-\hat{T}_o^{\dB}
    \right|,
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{peak}}
    =
    \cos^{-1}
    \left(
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{target\,peak}}
        \cdot
        \vect{n}_{\mathrm{predicted\,peak}}
    \right).
\end{equation}

Aerodynamic performance is tracked using integrated lift and drag proxies:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:aero_proxies}
    L^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{l,m}c_m\Delta s_m,\\
    D^*
    &=
    \sum_m
    \frac{1}{2}\rho_0U_\infty^2
    C_{d,m}c_m\Delta s_m,
\end{align}
and the ratio $(L/D)^* = L^*/D^*$. Baseline-relative retention ratios $R_L$, $R_D$, and $R_{L/D}$ measure the aerodynamic impact of structural mutations.

\subsection{Mechanism ablation and optimization campaign}

We isolate acoustic mechanisms by optimizing each target under four distinct configurations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Incidence only} (\texttt{incidence}): Spatial parameters are fixed; only the 7 incidence coefficients are optimized.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, zero coherence} (\texttt{zero\_coherence}): The full 18-variable vector is optimized, but $\Gamma_{mn} = \delta_{mn}$, disabling coherent phase-steering.
    \item \textbf{Full geometry, no delay} (\texttt{no\_delay}): The full 18-variable vector is optimized, but $\tau_\alpha = 0$, disabling incidence-delay phase shifts.
    \item \textbf{Full model} (\texttt{full}): The full 18-variable design space is optimized under the complete model.
\end{enumerate}

The genetic optimizer searches the 18 design variables using a differential-evolution population of size:
\begin{equation}
    P=N_pN_\mu,
\label{eq:population_size}
\end{equation}
where $N_\mu = 18$ and $N_p$ is the multiplier. Candidates are penalized for physical source-line intersections. We employ a two-stage evaluation: candidates are screened on a coarse grid, and the top fraction is evaluated at full resolution.

To accelerate the genetic search, the PyTorch acoustic path refactors the CSD matrix projection to scale transfer weights $\vect{a}_m$ directly by $\sqrt{\Phi_m}$. This changes the memory footprint from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$, and maps tensor contractions to hardware matrix units (AMX/MPS). For the \texttt{zero\_coherence} ablation, a specialized fast path skips distance and matrix calculations entirely, evaluating far-field pressure power as $S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega) = \sum_{m} |a_{m}|^2$.

\subsection{Statistical analysis}

We perform a paired repeated-target analysis across 50 random target seeds. Pairwise contrasts $d_s^{(A,B)} = y_s^{(A)} - y_s^{(B)}$ for response variables $y$ (validated RMSE, drag ratio, and $L/D$ retention) are evaluated using two-sided paired Student-$t$ tests. Family-wise error rates are controlled using Holm-adjusted $p$-values.

\section{Results}

Our 50-target optimization campaign (200 total runs across four configurations) shows that directivity control depends significantly on geometric configuration. We compare performance using final validated surrogate RMSE to avoid baseline biases across configurations.

\subsection{Acoustic performance and mechanism ablation}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Physical mechanisms of geometry-sensitive directivity control}

The primary result of our campaign is that full-geometry optimization achieves about $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ lower validated target-shape RMSE than incidence-only control, but the three full-geometry configurations (\texttt{full}, \texttt{no\_delay}, \texttt{zero\_coherence}) perform identically. 

To interpret this, we expand the far-field spectrum into diagonal (uncorrelated) and off-diagonal (coherent) terms:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \left[
        \sum_m |a_m|^2 \Phi_m
        +
        \sum_{m\neq n}
        a_m^* a_n
        \sqrt{\Phi_m\Phi_n}
        \Gamma_{mn}
    \right].
\end{equation}
The diagonal terms depend on local source amplitudes, loading orientations, and chords, while the off-diagonal terms introduce spatial coherence and delay phases. The equivalence of \texttt{zero\_coherence} ($\Gamma_{mn}=0$) and the other configurations indicates that the $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ performance improvement is driven by the diagonal term:
\begin{equation}
    S_{pp}^{(0)}(\vect{n},\omega)
    =
    \frac{1}{(4\pi R)^2}
    \sum_m
    \left|
        K_H(\vect{n},\omega)
        c_m
        (\vect{d}_m\cdot\vect{n})
        w_m
    \right|^2
    \Phi_m.
\end{equation}
Spatial re-spacing, sweep-induced local dipole rotation ($\vect{d}_m\cdot\vect{n}$), and source strength redistribution ($\Phi_m$) dominate over inter-feather phase interference. 

This is not because the phase-delay term is mathematically inactive. The optimized full-model configurations exhibit pairwise incidence spreads of $1.51$--$2.47\,\mathrm{rad}$ (median $1.94\,\mathrm{rad}$), yielding active delay phase groups $\Theta_{mn}=\omega b_\alpha(\alpha_m-\alpha_n)$ ranging from $1.52$ (at $250\,\mathrm{Hz}$) to $48.64$ (at $8000\,\mathrm{Hz}$). Rather, these phase effects remain secondary to geometric dipole projections and source weighting in this regime.

\subsection{Limitations and model bounds}

The theory--surrogate discrepancy increases significantly above $\kappa = kc_{\mathrm{ref}} \approx 11$ ($2\,\mathrm{kHz}$). This spectral discrepancy arises because the single-frequency calibration does not align the distinct spectral envelopes of the compact theoretical autospectrum and the multi-component, BPM/TNO-inspired surrogate. 

Therefore, the present framework serves as a relative screening and mechanism-isolation tool for low-to-mid-frequency directivity matching. It is not suitable for high-frequency absolute sound level predictions. Further calibration against high-fidelity experimental or computational datasets is required to establish physical closure parameters.

\section{Conclusion}

We have presented a positive-semidefinite stochastic array framework to analyze feathered-wingtip directivity control. By using a Hermitian cross-spectral matrix, the formulation guarantees physically valid, non-negative pressure spectra while enabling comparative mechanism ablations. 

In a 50-target optimization campaign, modifying full geometry achieved a $1.3\,\mathrm{dB}$ validated RMSE advantage over incidence-only control. However, the identical performance of the \texttt{full}, \texttt{no\_delay}, and \texttt{zero\_coherence} models indicates that this improvement is driven by geometric source redistribution and dipole rotation rather than coherent phase steering. Validation against an independent self-noise surrogate indicates that the model is reliable for low-to-mid-frequency screening ($\kappa \le 11$). Future work will focus on calibrating the closure constants using high-fidelity aerodynamic and acoustic data.

\section{Evaluation of the PSD Matrix}

The following pseudocode outlines the implementation used to evaluate the far-field pressure power spectral density across a set of observer directions and frequencies. It illustrates how the double-summation structure and local spanwise numerical integration are aggregated.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Evaluation of Positive-Semidefinite Array Far-Field Pressure Spectrum}
\label{alg:psd_eval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Observer set $\{\mathbf{n}\}$, Frequency set $\{\omega\}$, Array geometry $\{\mathbf{r}_i(\eta), \mathbf{l}_i(\eta), J_i(\eta), \alpha_i\}$, Integration nodes $\{\eta_q\}$ and weights $\{w_q\}$
\Ensure Far-field acoustic pressure power spectral density $S_{pp}(\mathbf{n}, \omega)$
\For{each frequency $\omega$}
    \For{each observer $\mathbf{n}$}
        \State Initialize total PSD: $S_{pp} \gets 0$
        \For{each feather $m \in \{1 \dots N\}$}
            \For{each feather $n \in \{1 \dots N\}$}
                \State Compute coherence decay $\Gamma_{mn}$ based on spatial separation $|\mathbf{r}_m - \mathbf{r}_n|$
                \State Compute incidence-dependent aerodynamic phase delay $\tau_{\alpha}$
                \State Form complex cross-term correlation: $C_{mn} \gets \Gamma_{mn} \exp(-\ii \omega \tau_{\alpha})$
                \State Initialize spanwise integral term: $I_{mn} \gets 0$
                \For{each integration node $\eta_q$}
                    \State Compute transfer $H_m \propto \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}_m(\eta_q) \exp(\ii k \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r}_m(\eta_q))$
                    \State Compute transfer $H_n^* \propto \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}_n(\eta_q) \exp(-\ii k \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{r}_n(\eta_q))$
                    \State Retrieve local source autospectra $\Phi_{qq}^{mm}(\eta_q)$ and $\Phi_{qq}^{nn}(\eta_q)$
                    \State Calculate cross-source amplitude $A \gets \sqrt{\Phi_{qq}^{mm} \Phi_{qq}^{nn}}$
                    \State Accumulate: $I_{mn} \gets I_{mn} + H_m H_n^* A J_m(\eta_q) J_n(\eta_q) w_q$
                \EndFor
                \State Add to total observer spectrum: $S_{pp} \gets S_{pp} + C_{mn} I_{mn}$
            \EndFor
        \EndFor
        \State Normalize geometrically: $S_{pp} \gets S_{pp} / (4\pi R)^2$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \Return $S_{pp}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
